8. Moves

A move is a package of rules with a trigger written in plain language: "When you shape arcane power while sparing the living land…", "When you approach a beast calmly and show no fear…". Moves come in two mechanical forms.

Rollable moves

A rollable move names a stat, a default difficulty, and a results block. To use one:

  1. Trigger it in the fiction. Describe your character doing the thing. The table agrees the move fires. (You can't invoke the move's name as an action — the description comes first.)
  2. Build the pool (chapter 4). The move's printed difficulty is the default; the GM adjusts it for the situation, then adds boosts and setbacks from the fiction. On the web sheet, the move's Roll button does all of this — set difficulty, add blue/black dice, click.
  3. Read the roll against the move's results block (chapter 5):
  • the success line — what you get;
  • the failure line — what goes wrong instead;
  • the ▲ menu — options to buy with net advantage. This is where the move's hold currency is earned, if it has one ("Hold 1 String…");
  • the ▼ menu — costs the GM picks from with net threat;
  • the ◆ line and ✶ line — what a triumph or despair means here.
  1. Say what it looks like. The fiction changes; play continues.

If the dice give you something the menus don't cover, fall back to the exchange rates in chapter 5.

Common action moves

Most playbook moves are written on your sheet, but the following procedures come up at every table.

Risky check. When danger looms, the stakes are high, and no named move fits, roll the stat that matches your approach (chapter 4). On success, you do it. On threat, the GM gives a lesser success, cost, consequence, or hard choice. On failure, mark XP and the GM makes a move.

Clash. When you fight in melee or close quarters, roll with the stat that fits the weapon and approach, usually STR. On success, deal your damage. On advantage, avoid, prevent, or counter the enemy's attack, or strike hard for an extra d6 damage while still suffering their attack. On threat, you suffer the enemy's attack or another immediate cost. On failure, the enemy has the upper hand.

Let fly. When you take an easy shot with a ranged weapon and the fiction gives you a clear line, deal your damage. If the shot is tricky or you are under pressure, roll DEX. On success, deal your damage. On threat, choose or accept a cost: deplete ammo, wait for a clear shot, move into danger for the shot, or rush it and accept the GM's complication.

Defend. When you take up a defensive stance or throw yourself between a threat and your ward, roll CON. On advantage, hold Readiness; a shield gives more. Spend Readiness one-for-one to suffer an attack's damage or effects instead of your ward, halve an attack's effect or damage, draw attention to yourself, or strike back with disadvantage on your damage roll. When you stop defending or the threat passes, lose unspent Readiness.

Static moves

A move with plain text and no stat doesn't roll — it is a standing permission, truth, or price. When its trigger happens, do what it says. Many static moves trade a track mark for an effect ("add 1 Debt and the patron's door opens") — that trade is the move. Others simply make something always true for you that isn't true for anyone else.

The three kinds on your sheet

  • Signature — your archetype's defining move, always on, usually carrying a named hold (chapter 9).
  • Fixed — always on; you have all of them from the start.
  • Choice — you have the ones you've checked; advancement adds more.

Helping and hindering

Aid: if your bond, position, or skill makes the help credible, describe how you help and give the roller one blue die. You are in the scene now — the GM may spend threat from that roll against you.

Interfere: describe how you get in someone's way and impose one black die on their roll — and accept the fictional consequences of being in the way.

Both apply to other player characters and to NPCs alike. Only one aid die and one interference die per source, and the fiction must carry it.
