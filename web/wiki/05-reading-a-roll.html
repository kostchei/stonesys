5. Reading a Roll

Read every roll in the same order.

1. Did it work?

Cancel each ☓ against a ✱. One or more surviving ✱ = the task works. Zero = it doesn't. (◆ counts one ✱ toward this; ✶ counts one ☓.)

On a failure, mark 1 XP (chapter 11). Failing is how your character grows — the dice never just say "no," they say "no, and here's trouble, and here's experience."

2. Triumph and despair fire — no matter what

Every ◆ and every ✶ on the table happens. They are their own axis, separate from success and separate from each other. Read the move's triumph or despair line, or let the GM interpret.

3. Read the texture

Cancel ▼ against ▲. Whatever survives is the roll's texture, in tiers:

  • 0 net — none. Clean and quiet.
  • 1–2 net — minor. One ripple: a small gain or a small cost.
  • 3 or more net — major. A real turn: a big gain or a real price.

What to expect

"Success, but…" and "miss with a silver lining" are normal results, not edge cases. At middling stats, a costly success is the most common kind of success, and a good share of misses still hand you an advantage to spend. Expect texture on most rolls; clean-and-quiet is the rare one. Trained characters see a triumph roughly one risky roll in twelve — masters, twice that — and every risky roll carries about the same chance of despair. Play long enough and both will find you.

Spending what the dice gave you

Most moves print a menu: "on ▲, choose…", "on ▼, the GM chooses…". Use the menu first. When the menu doesn't cover what the dice gave you, use the default exchange rates:

Dice resultBuys
1–2 ▲ (minor)one small menu item, or a blue die to the next ally acting on this
3+ ▲ (major)one big item, or two small, or hold 1 of the move's currency (chapter 9)
◆the move's triumph line, or hold 1 and a small item
1–2 ▼ (minor)one small cost: a black die soon, lose a consumable, a detail turns sour
3+ ▼ (major)a real cost: tick a track (chapters 12–15), lose your position, break the tool
✶the move's despair line, or the GM's hardest move — now

That "lose a consumable" line is how ammunition, torches, provisions, and remedies run out in this game: supply erosion is a threat spend, not bookkeeping.
