4. Making a Check

When a move tells you to roll, you build a pool: positive dice on your side, negative dice on the task's side, all thrown together.

If danger looms, the stakes are high, and no named move fits, make a risky check. Choose the stat that matches how you face the danger:

  • STR to power through or test your might.
  • DEX to rely on speed, agility, aim, or finesse.
  • CON to endure, hold steady, or keep going.
  • INT to apply expertise or execute a clever plan.
  • WIS to rely on senses, judgment, nerve, or will.
  • CHA to charm, bluff, impress, command, or fit in.

The GM sets the difficulty from the situation, usually average. A risky check normally has no move training, but the risk rule below still guarantees at least one yellow and one red die.

Step 1 — Greens from your stat

Every character has six stats — STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA — each rated 1 to 5 (chapter 6). The move names the stat it uses. Take green Ability dice equal to that stat. WIS 3 means three greens when a move says "roll +WIS."

Step 2 — Upgrade for training

If you have the move on your sheet, you are trained in it: upgrade one green die to a yellow Proficiency die — swap it out, don't add. A rare advancement can raise a move to two upgrades, but the pool can never hold more than 2 yellow dice.

Step 3 — The GM sets difficulty

The GM adds purple Difficulty dice for how hard the task is in the current situation:

DifficultyPurple diceFeels like
Simple0riskless chores — usually no roll at all
Easy1favorable conditions
Average2the default
Hard3serious opposition or bad conditions
Daunting4near the edge of possible

Each move prints a default difficulty; the GM adjusts it from the fiction.

Step 4 — The risk rule

Moves are risky by default: when real stakes ride on the roll, the pool must contain at least one yellow and at least one red die. If you're untrained, upgrade a green to yellow anyway. On the negative side, upgrade one purple to a red Challenge die. When the stakes are real, something wonderful and something terrible are always possible — that's what the yellow and red dice carry (◆ and ✶ live only there).

The GM can upgrade a second purple to red when the opposition is truly dangerous, but the pool never holds more than 2 red dice.

Step 5 — Boosts and setbacks from the fiction

  • Add 1 blue Boost die for each meaningful edge: the right tool, high ground, surprise, a true insight, an ally's aid.
  • Add 1 black Setback die for each meaningful hindrance: poor light, no footing, improvised gear, being watched, someone interfering.

Blues and blacks never cancel each other before the roll — throw them all and let the dice sort it out.

The rails

Three limits hold every pool, always:

  • No side ever exceeds 5 dice (blues and blacks don't count against this; greens/yellows and purples/reds do).
  • Never more than 2 yellow.
  • Never more than 2 red.

Nothing breaks the rails — not Story Points, not talents, not stacked advantages. If an effect would push past a cap, it becomes a blue (or black) die instead.

Then roll

Throw the whole pool at once and read it as chapter 5 describes.

Example. Vesh (CHA 4) talks his way past a suspicious guard,
triggering a move he's trained in. Positive side: 4 greens for CHA, one
upgraded for training → 3 green + 1 yellow. The GM calls the task
average and it's clearly risky: 2 purples, one upgraded → **1 purple +
1 red**. Vesh knows the guard's name from an earlier scene — a true
insight — so +1 blue. He rolls 3G 1Y 1B against 1P 1R.
