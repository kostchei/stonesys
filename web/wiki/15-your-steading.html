15. Your Steading

The party's home — the steading — is a shared character with its own sheet. A steading might be a village, a war-camp, a tribe's holt, a cellar den, a tower, a ship. Its sheet carries what the place is and what it can do.

The steading sheet

  • Ratings, each from −1 to +3:
  • Scale — size, reach, and population.
  • Safety — walls, guards, secrecy, law, defensibility.
  • Prosperity — money, trade, harvest, craft, comfort.
  • Influence — reputation, political reach, faith, fear, alliances.
  • Supply — stores, tools, food, medicine, repair capacity.
  • Facilities — the working parts of the place (a forge, an infirmary, a shrine, a hidden room…). Each names its function, which rating it uses, one order it performs especially well, and one vulnerability.
  • Named people — the NPCs your community ties point at.
  • Trouble (0–4) — pressure on the steading: 0 stable · 1 complaints, shortages, rumors · 2 open problem · 3 crisis · 4 disaster. At Trouble 4 the crisis breaks and reshapes the situation; afterward Trouble resets to 2.
  • Fortunes (1–5) — the steading's confidence and goodwill, rolled as a pool on season moves and steading turns, exactly like a stat.
  • Surplus — the steading's hold currency: stores put by. Spend it to feed the winter, fund projects, or trade. Surplus 0 in winter is a crisis.

Some steadings also hold a strange resource — a rare, dangerous asset (true steel, old magic, a secret way). Its entry says what it grants and what it draws.

When the steading exploits a strange resource, add +1 Prosperity while the resource flows. If the steading fails a Gather, Craft, Trade, or Build order while exploiting it, the GM chooses or rolls a side effect:

d10Resource side effect
1Workers develop marks, mutations, dreams, or compulsions.
2A faction demands exclusive rights.
3A god, spirit, or monster claims the resource as sacred.
4The resource changes a facility.
5Local animals, crops, or weather become strange.
6A cure, stabilizer, or protective charm becomes necessary.
7Smugglers and raiders arrive.
8The resource empowers enemies as easily as allies.
9The supply is richer but more dangerous than expected.
10The resource awakens something below.

The season move

Once each season, the steading rolls for how the season goes:

  • Positive: green dice equal to Fortunes, upgraded once if a facility clearly leads the season's work. Season rolls are always risky — the year always carries a yellow and a red.
  • Negative: difficulty set by the season — spring average, deep winter hard or worse.

Read it on the grid as ever: ▲ can be spent for Surplus; ▼ ticks Trouble; ◆ is a windfall — an alliance, a birth, a discovery; ✶ is disaster — raid, fire, plague.

Steading turns

When a week of downtime passes, after an expedition, or when the GM says the wider world has moved, the steading may take a turn:

  1. Choose one order from the table below.
  2. Choose one facility to lead it.
  3. Build the pool: greens equal to the rating the order uses (if the rating is negative, roll 0 greens and add 1 black die). Default difficulty average (2).
  4. Roll and apply. Success with ▲: clean, plus one extra benefit. Success with ▼: it works, but choose one cost. Failure: it fails or succeeds at a serious price.
  5. The world moves too — expect one external pressure to advance.

The steading and the party

The steading creates adventures; it doesn't replace them. If home can solve a problem alone, let it. When it can't, the turn's result reveals what the characters must do in person — and that's next session. Your Standing (0–3) with the steading, where your campaign uses it, measures how hard the community will rally for you in particular.
