19. The Hollow-Oak Holt

The premise

Deep in an old forest, sheltered in a fold of hills, a small tribe of elves lives in and around a single enormous, ancient hollow oak — dens and platforms grown and woven into the great tree, wolf-trails threading out to the hunting grounds, smoke curling from the Mother Tree's heart. The world is hardening: each winter runs longer, humans push at the wood's edge, and the game grows thin. Of late the tribe has taken in an outsider or two.

The magic here is nobody's loan. It is in the blood and the land — shapers who coax living wood and stone, healers who mend flesh with their hands, senders who speak mind to mind — and each generation shapes a little less, remembers a little less, while the cold presses a little closer.

Your home

The steading is the Holt (Scale 0, Safety +1, Prosperity 0, Influence −1, Supply +1), with two facilities: the Mother Tree, shelter and heart of the tribe, which hides and warms everyone through the worst weather but cannot forgive fire; and the Shaping-Grove, where the shapers work wood, stone, and flesh — the tribe's craft, healing, and quiet magic, resting on a very few gifted hands.

The Holt's strange resource is the old magic in the land itself: it cannot be mined or hoarded, only lived with and tended — and it draws the notice of humans (as devilry) and of older things that remember when the magic was stronger.

Embedment style

Village. No patron, god, or bastion tracks — no pips at all on the embedment rail. The bonds are the embedment: recognition, sending, pack and tribe all live in the fill-in statements between characters and in each playbook's community tie to the Holt. Chapters 12–14 are not used in this campaign; the steading rules (chapter 15) very much are.

The nine playbooks

Troll Weaponsmith

You are a troll, and trolls make the finest weapons in the world — everyone knows it, which is why the elves let you stay even though half of them will never trust a troll. You came to the Hollow-Oak as an outsider and, slowly, became something like kin. Your hammer keeps the tribe armed against a world that is closing in.

Duty: Your craft is why they keep you, and never quite why they trust you. Every blade you forge is a bond you're building, one strike at a time.

At the Forge · Signature

When you forge, mend, or improve arms and gear at a hot fire.

Roll +STR · default difficulty average.

  • Success: You make or mend the thing the tribe needs, and it is good work.
  • Failure: It won't come right — bad metal, a hidden flaw, or the work costs more than you have.
  • ▲ spend: Hold 1 Craftwork (spend: a masterwork edge — +1 damage or a boost die with it / a hidden feature built in / mend it fast, right now / enough to arm several of the tribe). / You learn something true about the metal, or about who made a blade before you.
  • ▼ costs: It needs rare materials, or more time than you'll easily find. / The fire, the noise, or the smoke draws unwanted eyes.
  • ◆ Triumph: A masterwork that will outlive you and become a story the tribe tells.
  • ✶ Despair: The work fails and wastes precious metal, or the forge-fire brings danger down on the Holt.

Hold — Craftwork. Spend 1 to: A masterwork edge: +1 damage, or a boost die when the piece is used. / A hidden feature — a concealed blade, a catch, a mark only you know. / Mend it fast, right now, in the middle of trouble. / Turn out enough to arm several of the tribe at once.

Troll-Made · Fixed

Because trolls make the finest weapons, and you are a troll among elves:

Your work is prized and famous; a blade of your making opens doors and earns respect. But your outsider-ness is never forgotten — some elves will always see the troll first and the kin second, and other trolls may name you a traitor for making arms for elves.

Desert Elf

You come from a far people — sun-baked, patient, settled around water in a land the forest elves have never seen. Chance or grief or a long journey brought you to the Hollow-Oak, and you stayed. You endure what would break them, and you see the things they have grown too used to notice. To the tribe you are half-marvel, half-stranger.

Duty: You are new here, from a people they barely believe in. Your patience and your outside eyes are gifts the tribe does not yet know how to value.

Chieftain

The tribe is yours to lead, which means it is yours to keep alive. Every mouth, every cub, every quarrel and hunt and hard winter comes home to you. You did not ask to carry them all, or perhaps you fought for the right. Either way the lodge is yours now, and the weight of it never sets down.

Duty: Lead, and they live; falter, and they scatter or starve. The tribe's fate is the measure of you, and it is never off your shoulders.

The Tribe Looks to You · Fixed

Because every life in the Holt is yours to answer for:

When you spend yourself for the tribe — take the hard watch, the hard choice, the blame — they trust you the more for it, and rally to you when it counts. But you carry them always; their losses are your wounds, and you cannot set the weight down while you hold the lodge.

Healer

Yours is the magic of the living body — to knit the torn, cool the fever, coax a failing heart to beat on. In a small tribe pressed by a hard world, that gift is the thin line between the Holt and the long silence. They cannot lose you, and every one of them knows it, and so do you.

Duty: Your hands are the tribe's shield against the wounds of the hunt and the wasting of the cold. What you can heal, you must; what you cannot, you carry.

Tree-Shaper

The living wood answers you. Under your hands a sapling becomes a shelter, a branch becomes a bow, a wall of thorn rises between the tribe and the dark. You did not learn this so much as remember it, the way the forest remembers you. The Mother Tree itself is half your doing, and half your teacher.

Duty: The green is not a tool but a partner; it gives as you give, and it remembers every cut. Shape with the forest, never merely against it.

The Deep Bones · Fixed

Because you feel the stone and earth around you always:

You sense the rock's faults, hollows, veins, and hidden ways — take a boost die when that closeness serves you, and no deep or dark place of stone is ever wholly blind to you. But the deep patience of the stone has settled into you; you are slow to act in haste, and quick things sometimes leave you behind.
