# Stonetop Mechanics Research

Research date: 2026-06-29

This is a design-focused summary of the most important Stonetop mechanics. It is not a rules replacement.

## What Stonetop Is

Stonetop is a hearth fantasy tabletop RPG by Jeremy Strandberg with art, layout, art direction, and publishing by Jason Lutes / Lampblack & Brimstone. It is built in the Powered by the Apocalypse lineage, with clear influence from Dungeon World, but its core focus is different from wandering-adventurer fantasy.

The central premise is that the player characters are important people from a specific isolated village. Adventures matter because they affect home, family, neighbors, obligations, survival, and the village's long-term future.

## Core Loop

Stonetop is played as a conversation:

1. The GM establishes a situation.
2. Players ask questions, clarify, and say what their characters do.
3. If a move is triggered by the fiction, the table follows that move.
4. If no move is triggered, the GM says what happens based on the fiction.
5. The result changes the situation, and the conversation continues.

The most important design principle is that mechanics are triggered by fictional positioning. A player does not press a "move button." The group agrees that the character's fictional action matches a move trigger, then resolves it.

## Move Results

Most player-facing rolls follow the familiar PbtA result spread:

- 10+ is a strong hit.
- 7-9 is a weak hit, partial success, success with cost, or constrained choice.
- 6- is a miss. In Stonetop, a miss generally grants XP unless the move says otherwise, and the GM makes a hard move or otherwise says what bad thing happens.

Important design effect: the system does not treat low rolls as dead ends. A miss creates experience and pushes the fiction forward, usually through danger, cost, revelation, or escalation.

## Fictional Positioning

Stonetop places heavy emphasis on what is actually true in the fiction:

- What is within reach?
- What tools or leverage does the character have?
- What danger is already established?
- What can one move reasonably accomplish?
- Is the character acting in a scene or in looser zoomed-out play?
- Does the action trigger a move, require some prerequisite, or simply happen?

This makes the conversation before the roll as important as the roll itself. The roll resolves uncertainty inside an already-established fictional situation.

Design takeaway: if adapting Stonetop ideas, build procedures that make players clarify intent, method, risk, and stakes before dice hit the table.

## GM Role

The GM normally does not roll dice and does not trigger player moves. The GM presents situations, asks what the characters do, follows the rules when moves trigger, and makes soft or hard GM moves as appropriate.

Soft GM moves show pressure and invite response. Hard GM moves establish consequences, often after a player ignores danger or rolls a miss.

This keeps player action at the center of resolution. Danger is not hidden inside GM attack rolls; it is established in the fiction and resolved through the players' responses.

## Player Characters

Stonetop uses playbooks, each representing a distinct kind of protagonist with a reason to care about the village. The official overview describes nine player-character types.

The important mechanical function of playbooks is not only niche definition. They also tie PCs to the community, local NPCs, home obligations, and long-term concerns.

Design takeaway: a playbook can encode social position, obligations, and home ties, not just powers and combat role.

## The Village As A Mechanical Focus

The village of Stonetop is not just backdrop. It has its own playbook and can change across seasons and years. The game includes rules for improving the village, responding to disasters, and tracking how adventures shape home life.

Important effects:

- The campaign has a persistent center of gravity.
- Success and failure affect people the PCs know.
- Downtime and home scenes matter mechanically.
- Long-term development is not limited to character advancement.

Design takeaway: make the community a stateful game object. Track its resources, vulnerabilities, improvements, relationships, and consequences.

## Modes Of Play

Stonetop supports multiple play modes with different move sets and procedures:

- Scene-level play for immediate action and dialogue.
- Loose play for broader time, travel, investigation, and montage.
- Expedition procedures for travel beyond the village.
- Homestead procedures for life in Stonetop, projects, trouble, recovery, and communal work.
- Site and danger procedures for exploration and threats.

This is one of the most important structural innovations. The game does not rely on one universal procedure for everything. It changes resolution texture depending on whether the characters are at home, on the road, in danger, or dealing with long-term consequences.

Design takeaway: explicitly separate adventure, travel, home, downtime, and seasonal procedures.

## Expeditions And Travel

Travel is a major part of Stonetop. The official overview highlights overland travel rules that emphasize the danger and wonder of leaving home and the relief of returning.

Important functions:

- The world beyond home feels uncertain and costly.
- Provisions and preparation matter.
- The road can generate discovery, depletion, danger, and character interaction.
- Return home is structurally meaningful, not just a skipped transition.

Design takeaway: travel should have procedure, risk, and emotional contrast. It should not be only a distance tax.

## Gear And Inventory

Stonetop treats gear and inventory as meaningful enough to have a dedicated visual tutorial in the official materials. The public commentary around the game also notes a tight provisions economy.

The important design idea is that equipment, load, supplies, and readiness affect what actions are feasible. Gear is not only a shopping list; it shapes fictional positioning.

Design takeaway: inventory should answer "what can you plausibly do right now?" and "what did preparation cost?"

## Hold And Spend

Some moves generate a named temporary currency that can be spent later for specific effects. The currency is tied to the move and the fiction, such as readiness, preparation, or a similar move-specific resource.

Important effects:

- A good roll can create delayed agency.
- The player gets permission to interrupt or redirect danger later.
- The currency remains grounded because it must be described in the fiction when spent.

Design takeaway: temporary move currencies are useful when a character establishes readiness, leverage, insight, or preparation that should matter later in the scene.

## Harm And Recovery

Stonetop retains recognizable fantasy adventure stakes such as hit points, damage, debilities, wounds, and recovery, but places them inside a broader structure of home, healing, and consequences.

The important design point is that harm is not isolated from campaign life. Being injured, depleted, or marked by events should affect the ability to protect the village and engage with obligations.

## Progression

Stonetop uses XP, and misses generally award XP. Character improvement exists alongside village development. This creates two advancement tracks:

- The characters change through experience.
- The community changes through projects, seasons, disasters, and improvements.

Design takeaway: dual advancement makes the campaign about both personal growth and collective survival.

## Most Important Mechanics To Borrow

For StoneSys or any design influenced by Stonetop, the highest-value mechanics are:

1. Fiction-triggered moves: mechanics fire from concrete fictional action.
2. Three-band roll results: strong hit, weak hit, miss with forward motion.
3. Miss XP: failure teaches and pushes play forward.
4. GM does not usually roll: pressure comes from established fiction and GM moves.
5. Community as character: the village has state, needs, improvements, and consequences.
6. Home/adventure loop: dangerous expeditions are bookended by scenes that show what the PCs are fighting for.
7. Distinct procedures by mode: home, expedition, scenes, sites, dangers, seasons, and downtime should not all resolve the same way.
8. Gear as positioning: inventory affects what is possible, not only numeric bonuses.
9. Hold-and-spend currencies: moves can create specific stored agency.
10. Playbooks as obligations: character types should encode relationships and responsibilities, not just powers.
