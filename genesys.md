# Genesys Mechanics Research

Research date: 2026-06-29

This is a design-focused summary of the most important Genesys mechanics. It is not a rules replacement.

## What Genesys Is

Genesys is Fantasy Flight Games / Edge Studio's generic roleplaying system built around the Narrative Dice System. Its central promise is setting flexibility: the same core engine supports fantasy, modern investigation, science fiction, pulp, horror, romance, superheroes, vehicle combat, hacking, magic, and other modular subsystems.

The key design idea is that a roll should answer more than "do you succeed?" It should also create side effects, complications, openings, costs, and tonal shifts.

## Core Resolution

Genesys uses custom symbol dice assembled into a dice pool.

Important dice categories:

- Positive capability dice come from characteristics, skills, help, gear, positioning, talents, and favorable circumstances.
- Negative challenge dice come from task difficulty, opposition, risk, environment, and complications.
- Dice can be upgraded into stronger positive or negative dice, which introduces the possibility of exceptional positive or negative outcomes.
- Boost and setback dice model short-term fictional edges or hindrances.

The pool produces several result axes:

- Success vs. failure determines whether the attempted task works.
- Advantage vs. threat determines beneficial or harmful side effects independent of success.
- Triumph and despair create major positive or negative turns and can appear even alongside an opposite basic result.

This means the system supports outcomes like:

- Success with threat: the character gets what they wanted, but pays a cost or creates a new problem.
- Failure with advantage: the character fails the task, but learns something, creates an opening, protects an ally, or improves the next attempt.
- Success with triumph: the task works and the scene changes strongly in the characters' favor.
- Failure with despair: the task fails and the fiction turns sharply worse.

For design purposes, the most important feature is the split between task success and narrative consequence. The dice do not only adjudicate competence. They produce prompts for collaborative scene evolution.

## Dice Pool Construction

For a skill check, Genesys combines a characteristic and a skill. The higher value sets the number of base positive dice, and the lower value upgrades that many of those dice into stronger dice. Difficulty adds negative dice, and circumstances can add boost or setback dice.

This makes attributes and training both matter, but in different ways:

- A naturally gifted but untrained character rolls a wider basic pool.
- A trained character upgrades dice and gains access to stronger symbols.
- Difficulty is not just a target number; it actively rolls against the character.

Design takeaway: opposing dice make risk visible before the roll. The table sees not only "target number 3" but the actual fictional pressure in the pool.

## Story Points

Story Points are a shared table currency split between the player side and the GM side. Players can spend from their pool to upgrade their checks or affect the fiction. When one side spends a point, it moves to the other side.

Important effects:

- They create a visible rhythm of dramatic pressure.
- Player empowerment feeds later GM pressure, and GM escalation feeds later player opportunity.
- They are best used at dramatic moments rather than as routine numeric bonuses.

Design takeaway: the economy is adversarial only in the lightest sense. It is really a pacing valve for spotlight, risk, and narrative permission.

## Characters

Character creation is intentionally broad:

- Start with a background and concept.
- Pick an archetype or species that establishes base characteristics and unique traits.
- Pick a career, which defines cheaper career skills without locking the character into a rigid class.
- Spend experience on characteristics, skills, and talents.
- Calculate derived attributes such as wound threshold, strain threshold, defense, and soak.
- Define motivations, usually including desire, fear, strength, and flaw.
- Buy starting gear appropriate to the setting.

The important structure is "guided freedom." Careers point characters toward an identity but do not fully define advancement.

## Skills And Careers

Skills are the default way characters interact with uncertainty. Careers make some skills cheaper to improve, but characters can still buy non-career skills. This keeps niche protection soft rather than absolute.

Design takeaway: this is useful for genre-flexible games because the career is a character development nudge, not a permission gate.

## Talents

Talents are special abilities, techniques, and exceptions that make characters distinct. Genesys uses tiered talents rather than fixed class trees. Higher-tier talents require a broader base of lower-tier talents, creating a pyramid-like advancement structure.

Important effects:

- Characters can customize toward a concept.
- Powerful abilities are gated by breadth, not by a predetermined class path.
- The system avoids locking every build into a fixed tree.

Design takeaway: a talent pyramid is a strong fit when we want flexible builds but still need pacing control.

## Harm, Strain, And Consequences

Genesys separates physical harm and pressure:

- Wounds represent physical injury and can incapacitate characters.
- Strain represents stress, fatigue, exertion, mental pressure, and resource burn.
- Critical injuries create lasting or escalated harm.
- Soak, defense, range bands, maneuvers, and actions structure combat without turning every exchange into a simple hit-point trade.

Strain is especially important because it doubles as a cost for effort, extra maneuvers, talents, and dramatic overextension.

Design takeaway: strain gives the system a way to charge characters for pushing beyond normal limits without always escalating to injury.

## Structured Encounters

Genesys can use structured time for combat and significant social encounters.

Notable features:

- Turns usually include an action and a maneuver.
- Characters can suffer strain to do more.
- Initiative creates player and NPC slots, and each side can choose who uses its available slots.
- Social scenes can also use structured turns when the exchange is complex or contested.
- Motivations can matter mechanically in social play by making persuasion easier or harder depending on what the character cares about.

Design takeaway: initiative slots are a good way to preserve tactical rhythm while letting the group coordinate dynamically.

## GM And System Design Tools

Genesys expects the GM or setting designer to tune the system:

- Choose or create the setting frame.
- Decide which skills, gear, archetypes, careers, talents, and optional subsystems apply.
- Use threat, advantage, triumph, and despair to keep scenes moving.
- Treat dice results as prompts for consequence, not only as numeric outputs.

The system is strongest when the table has shared expectations about tone. The same dice engine can support pulp heroics or gritty consequences, but the interpretation of advantage, threat, triumph, and despair must match the intended genre.

## Most Important Mechanics To Borrow

For StoneSys or any design influenced by Genesys, the highest-value mechanics are:

1. Multi-axis resolution: separate "success" from "side effect."
2. Visible risk pool: build the roll out of capabilities, difficulty, and circumstances.
3. Upgrade mechanic: convert ordinary dice into more volatile dice instead of only adding bonuses.
4. Shared dramatic currency: use a back-and-forth table economy to pace spotlight and escalation.
5. Strain as spendable pressure: let characters overextend without every cost being physical harm.
6. Career skills as soft guidance: make archetypes matter without hard class locks.
7. Talent pyramid: gate powerful abilities through breadth of development.
8. Motivation in social play: make character drives mechanically relevant.
