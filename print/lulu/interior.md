# 1. Welcome

This is a tabletop roleplaying game. One player, the **Game Master** (GM),
describes a world and plays everyone in it except the heroes. Every other
player takes one hero — a **character** — and says what that character does.
The game is the conversation between you: the GM describes a situation, you
respond as your character, and together you find out what happens.

Most of the time, what happens is obvious and nobody rolls anything. When
your character attempts something risky — something that could go wrong in an
interesting way — you build a pool of dice, roll it, and read the result. The
dice don't just say whether you succeeded. They also say what it cost, what
you gained on the side, and whether the situation just turned sharply in your
favor or against you.

Your character is not a wandering adventurer from nowhere. Each campaign in
this book gives the party a shared home — a village, a war-camp, a tribe's
refuge, a rented cellar — called a **steading**, with people in it your
characters know and care about. You leave home to deal with threats and chase
opportunities, and what you do out there changes life back home. The steading
has its own sheet, its own dice rolls, and its own fortunes across the
seasons.

## What you need at the table

- **One set of narrative dice** — six special dice described in chapter 2 —
  or the web character sheet, which rolls virtual ones. You will also want an
  ordinary d6, d8, and d10 for damage.
- **A playbook sheet for each player.** A playbook is a ready-made character
  archetype — the campaign chapters in Part V each offer nine. Print the A3
  sheet or use the web sheet.
- **The steading sheet** for your campaign.
- **Two tokens** for Story Points (chapter 9). Start with one on the players'
  side of the table and one on the GM's side.

## Who does what

**You** describe what your character does, concretely, in the world: not "I
use my persuade ability" but "I lean across the table and remind him whose
coin paid for this room." You roll dice when the rules say to, answer the
GM's questions about your character, and mark the sheet when things change.

**The GM** describes everything else, plays every other person and creature,
sets how difficult tasks are, and — when the dice go against you — decides
what goes wrong. The GM is not your opponent. The GM's job is to make the
world honest: dangerous when it should be, generous when you've earned it.

**The rules** decide the moments in between: whether the leap succeeds,
whether the lie holds, what the magic costs. When a rule and the ongoing
story disagree about what makes sense, the story — what everyone has agreed
is true in the world — wins. That principle is called **fiction first**, and
it is the single most important rule in this book.

# 2. The Dice

This game uses six kinds of dice. Three are **positive** — they work for you.
Three are **negative** — they work against you. Every roll throws a handful
of both, together, and reads all of them at once.

| Die | Color | Sides | What it represents |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Ability** | green | d8 | your raw capability — the stat you're rolling |
| **Proficiency** | yellow | d12 | trained mastery; the only die that can show **◆ Triumph** |
| **Boost** | blue | d6 | a momentary edge: the right tool, high ground, a friend's help |
| **Difficulty** | purple | d8 | how hard the task is |
| **Challenge** | red | d12 | real peril; the only die that can show **✶ Despair** |
| **Setback** | black | d6 | a momentary hindrance: bad light, no footing, being watched |

You never add numbers on these dice. Their faces show **symbols**, and
symbols are all that matter.

## The symbols

There are four pairs. Within the first two pairs, opposites cancel each other
one for one.

- **✱ Success** (on green, yellow, and blue dice) and **☓ Failure** (on
  purple, red, and black dice). Each ☓ cancels one ✱. If at least one ✱
  survives, the task **works**. Otherwise it doesn't.
- **▲ Advantage** (positive dice) and **▼ Threat** (negative dice). Each ▼
  cancels one ▲. Whatever survives is the roll's *side effect* — good
  fortune around the edges of the task, or cost and complication. Advantage
  and threat are completely independent of success and failure: you can
  succeed and still owe a price, or fail and still catch a break.
- **◆ Triumph** appears only on the yellow die. It counts as one ✱ toward
  success, *and* it means something remarkable breaks your way — above and
  beyond the task at hand.
- **✶ Despair** appears only on the red die. It counts as one ☓ toward
  failure, *and* it means something goes memorably, dramatically wrong.

**◆ and ✶ are never cancelled.** Their success/failure contribution cancels
normally, but the triumph or despair itself always fires. A roll can succeed
and still contain a ✶; a roll can fail and still contain a ◆. When both
appear on one roll, both happen.

## Reading a handful

Roll every die in the pool together. Then:

1. Count surviving ✱ after cancellation. One or more = the task worked.
2. Count surviving ▲ or ▼ after cancellation. That's the side effect.
3. Note every ◆ and ✶. Each one fires, no matter what else the dice say.

That's the whole engine. Chapter 4 tells you which dice to pick up; chapter
5 tells you what the results mean at the table.

# 3. The Conversation

Play moves in a loop:

1. **The GM describes the situation.** Where you are, what's happening,
   what's threatening or tempting.
2. **You say what your character does** — concretely, in the world. Where
   you stand, what you touch, what you say.
3. **If what you describe triggers a move, the move happens.** A **move** is
   a rule with a trigger written into it: "*When you pass yourself off as
   someone you are not…*", "*When you fight in close quarters…*". If your
   description matches a trigger, that move's rules take over — usually you
   build a dice pool, roll, and read the result against the move's text.
   Chapter 8 covers moves in full.
4. **If no move triggers, the GM just says what happens.** Not everything is
   a roll. Climbing a ladder, buying bread, walking to the mill — chores
   succeed. Only risk rolls.
5. **The result changes the situation.** Go back to step 1.

## Action scenes

Fights, chases, escapes, rescues, and other action scenes still use the same
conversation. There is no tactical grid and no fixed round structure. The GM
keeps the spotlight moving toward the characters in the most immediate
danger, the characters whose choices can change the situation, and anyone
the table has not heard from in a while.

Movement is handled from the fiction:

- If nothing stops you, you get there. Cross the room, duck behind a cart,
  draw steel, or step through an open door.
- If the movement is dangerous, contested, or time-critical, make a risky
  check or trigger the move that fits. Running past a spear line, crossing
  open ground under arrows, leaping a burning ditch, or forcing your way
  through a panicked crowd is not free.
- Distance is descriptive, not measured in squares. Use the weapon and
  position words that matter in the scene: hand, close, reach, near, and far.
  A dagger can matter at hand range; a spear keeps danger at reach; a bow
  needs space and a plausible shot.

Do not wait for "your turn" to protect someone, flee, shout a warning, seize
an opening, or press an attack. Say what you do. If the fiction gives an
enemy the immediate edge, the GM says what happens or asks what you do under
pressure.

## Fiction first

You cannot press a move like a button. You cannot say "I use Read the Room."
You describe your character pausing in the doorway, reading the hall — and
*that* triggers the move. The move belongs to the story, not the other way
around.

What is true in the world decides everything about the roll before dice ever
hit the table:

- **Whether a move triggers at all.** No footing for a leap means no leap —
  no roll, just a fall or a rethink.
- **How difficult it is.** The GM sets difficulty from the situation
  (chapter 4).
- **What helps and hinders.** The right tool, surprise, an ally at your
  shoulder add blue dice; darkness, mud, and watching eyes add black ones.

Settle all of that in conversation *first*. Then roll.

## Honesty both ways

The conversation only works if everyone plays the world straight. Say what
your character actually does, not what result you want. The GM, in turn,
tells you what you can see coming: how hard something looks, what it might
cost, what the warning signs are. Dice are for the moments when honesty on
both sides still leaves the outcome uncertain.

# 4. Making a Check

When a move tells you to roll, you build a **pool**: positive dice on your
side, negative dice on the task's side, all thrown together.

If danger looms, the stakes are high, and no named move fits, make a
**risky check**. Choose the stat that matches how you face the danger:

- **STR** to power through or test your might.
- **DEX** to rely on speed, agility, aim, or finesse.
- **CON** to endure, hold steady, or keep going.
- **INT** to apply expertise or execute a clever plan.
- **WIS** to rely on senses, judgment, nerve, or will.
- **CHA** to charm, bluff, impress, command, or fit in.

The GM sets the difficulty from the situation, usually **average**. A risky
check normally has no move training, but the risk rule below still guarantees
at least one yellow and one red die.

## Step 1 — Greens from your stat

Every character has six **stats** — STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA — each
rated **1 to 5** (chapter 6). The move names the stat it uses. Take **green
Ability dice equal to that stat**. WIS 3 means three greens when a move says
"roll +WIS."

## Step 2 — Upgrade for training

If you **have the move** on your sheet, you are trained in it: **upgrade one
green die to a yellow Proficiency die** — swap it out, don't add. A rare
advancement can raise a move to two upgrades, but the pool can never hold
more than **2 yellow dice**.

## Step 3 — The GM sets difficulty

The GM adds **purple Difficulty dice** for how hard the task is in the
current situation:

| Difficulty | Purple dice | Feels like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Simple | 0 | riskless chores — usually no roll at all |
| Easy | 1 | favorable conditions |
| **Average** | **2** | **the default** |
| Hard | 3 | serious opposition or bad conditions |
| Daunting | 4 | near the edge of possible |

Each move prints a default difficulty; the GM adjusts it from the fiction.

## Step 4 — The risk rule

Moves are **risky** by default: when real stakes ride on the roll, the pool
must contain **at least one yellow and at least one red die**. If you're
untrained, upgrade a green to yellow anyway. On the negative side, upgrade
one purple to a **red Challenge die**. When the stakes are real, something
wonderful and something terrible are always possible — that's what the
yellow and red dice carry (◆ and ✶ live only there).

The GM can upgrade a second purple to red when the opposition is truly
dangerous, but the pool never holds more than **2 red dice**.

## Step 5 — Boosts and setbacks from the fiction

- Add **1 blue Boost die** for each meaningful edge: the right tool, high
  ground, surprise, a true insight, an ally's aid.
- Add **1 black Setback die** for each meaningful hindrance: poor light, no
  footing, improvised gear, being watched, someone interfering.

Blues and blacks never cancel each other before the roll — throw them all
and let the dice sort it out.

## The rails

Three limits hold every pool, always:

- **No side ever exceeds 5 dice** (blues and blacks don't count against
  this; greens/yellows and purples/reds do).
- **Never more than 2 yellow.**
- **Never more than 2 red.**

Nothing breaks the rails — not Story Points, not talents, not stacked
advantages. If an effect would push past a cap, it becomes a blue (or black)
die instead.

## Then roll

Throw the whole pool at once and read it as chapter 5 describes.

> **Example.** Vesh (CHA 4) talks his way past a suspicious guard,
> triggering a move he's trained in. Positive side: 4 greens for CHA, one
> upgraded for training → **3 green + 1 yellow**. The GM calls the task
> average and it's clearly risky: 2 purples, one upgraded → **1 purple +
> 1 red**. Vesh knows the guard's name from an earlier scene — a true
> insight — so **+1 blue**. He rolls 3G 1Y 1B against 1P 1R.

# 5. Reading a Roll

Read every roll in the same order.

## 1. Did it work?

Cancel each ☓ against a ✱. **One or more surviving ✱ = the task works.**
Zero = it doesn't. (◆ counts one ✱ toward this; ✶ counts one ☓.)

**On a failure, mark 1 XP** (chapter 11). Failing is how your character
grows — the dice never just say "no," they say "no, and here's trouble, and
here's experience."

## 2. Triumph and despair fire — no matter what

Every ◆ and every ✶ on the table happens. They are their own axis, separate
from success and separate from each other. Read the move's triumph or
despair line, or let the GM interpret.

## 3. Read the texture

Cancel ▼ against ▲. Whatever survives is the roll's texture, in tiers:

- **0 net** — none. Clean and quiet.
- **1–2 net** — **minor**. One ripple: a small gain or a small cost.
- **3 or more net** — **major**. A real turn: a big gain or a real price.

## The outcome grid

Every roll lands in one row:

| The dice say | Call it | What happens |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Success + net ▲ | **Success, and…** | The move's success line, plus you spend the advantage (the move's menu, or hold) |
| Success, net 0 | **Success** | The success line, clean and quiet |
| Success + net ▼ | **Success, but…** | You get it *and* you pay — the GM spends the threat from the move's menu |
| Failure + net ▲ | **Miss with a silver lining** | Mark XP; the GM moves against you, but softer — or you salvage something on the way down |
| Failure (net 0 or ▼) | **Miss** | Mark XP; the GM says what goes wrong |
| **◆ Triumph** (on any row) | **A turn in your favor** | The move's triumph line — even on a failure, something breaks your way |
| **✶ Despair** (on any row) | **A turn against you** | The move's despair line, or the GM's hardest move, right now — even on a success |

## What to expect

"Success, but…" and "miss with a silver lining" are **normal results, not
edge cases**. At middling stats, a costly success is the most common kind of
success, and a good share of misses still hand you an advantage to spend.
Expect texture on most rolls; clean-and-quiet is the rare one. Trained
characters see a triumph roughly one risky roll in twelve — masters, twice
that — and every risky roll carries about the same chance of despair. Play
long enough and both will find you.

## Spending what the dice gave you

Most moves print a menu: "on ▲, choose…", "on ▼, the GM chooses…". Use the
menu first. When the menu doesn't cover what the dice gave you, use the
default exchange rates:

| Dice result | Buys |
| --- | --- |
| 1–2 ▲ (minor) | one small menu item, or a blue die to the next ally acting on this |
| 3+ ▲ (major) | one big item, or two small, or **hold 1** of the move's currency (chapter 9) |
| ◆ | the move's triumph line, or hold 1 *and* a small item |
| 1–2 ▼ (minor) | one small cost: a black die soon, lose a consumable, a detail turns sour |
| 3+ ▼ (major) | a real cost: tick a track (chapters 12–15), lose your position, break the tool |
| ✶ | the move's despair line, or the GM's hardest move — now |

That "lose a consumable" line is how ammunition, torches, provisions, and
remedies run out in this game: supply erosion is a threat spend, not
bookkeeping.

> **Example, continued.** Vesh's pool (3G 1Y 1B vs 1P 1R) comes up: 2 ✱,
> 1 ▲, 1 ◆, 1 ▼. Success — the ✱ survive. The ▲ and ▼ cancel to zero:
> no texture. But the ◆ fires regardless. Read: the guard waves him
> through (the move's success line), *and* the move's triumph line —
> someone in authority takes a favorable interest in the stranger with the
> familiar manner. Clean success, plus a turn in his favor. The fiction
> moves.

# 6. The Character Sheet

Your sheet — printed A3 or the web version — has five zones. Read it top to
bottom, left to right.

## Header

The playbook's name, your character's name, your **Look** (a few chosen
descriptors), and the **duty line** in bold. The duty is not flavor text: it
is the standing pressure your archetype lives under — the choice or burden
that defines the role — and the GM will lean on it.

## Left rail — stats and vitals

**The six stats**, each rated 1–5:

| Stat | Covers |
| --- | --- |
| **STR** | strength: force, heavy lifting, raw muscle |
| **DEX** | dexterity: speed, stealth, aim, balance |
| **CON** | constitution: endurance, toughness, resisting harm and hardship |
| **INT** | intelligence: lore, logic, deciphering, wards and workings |
| **WIS** | wisdom: perception, instinct, sensing spirits and moods |
| **CHA** | charisma: persuasion, leadership, presence |

*The number is your dice.* WIS 3 means three green dice whenever a move says
"roll +WIS." The sheet prints the dice count under each stat.

Below the stats:

- **HP** — hit points, current and maximum. Your playbook sets the maximum
  (usually a base plus CON). When you take harm, HP goes down (chapter 10).
- **Damage die** — what you roll when the fiction says you hurt something
  (d6, d8, or d10, set by your playbook).
- **Load** — how much you can carry without slowing down, set by your
  playbook (usually a base plus STR).
- **XP** — experience marks (chapter 11).
- **Conditions** — written next to HP as you acquire them (chapter 9).

## Center — moves

Everything your character can do beyond the obvious, in three kinds:

- **Signature** (bordered): always on. The move that *is* your archetype,
  and usually the one that earns its named hold currency.
- **Fixed**: always on. Often plain text — a standing truth about your
  character rather than a roll.
- **Choice** (with checkboxes): you have the ones that are checked; take
  more through advancement.

Chapter 8 explains how to use them.

## Right rail — embedment

Who your character is entangled with. Depending on the campaign this shows:

- **Power-structure tracks** — rows of pips for Bond/Debt/Heat with a
  patron, Devotion/Favor/Wrath with a god, Standing with your steading, or a
  personal track such as Defilement or Corruption (Parts III–IV). Each row's
  note states its threshold rule.
- **Bonds** — fill-in-the-blank statements naming other player characters:
  "____ fought beside me when it counted." Bonds say who you are to each
  other, and resolving one earns XP.
- **Community tie** — your personal hook into the steading: a person, a
  place, or a debt that makes home *yours*.

Some campaigns (the Hollow-Oak Holt) have no tracks at all — the bonds *are*
the embedment.

## Footer — advancement and gear

The **advancement checklist** (chapter 11) and your **gear picks** — the
equipment choices your playbook offered at creation, plus whatever you've
gained since.

# 7. Creating a Character

Do this together, as a table, at the start of the campaign. It takes about
half an hour.

## 1. Pick your campaign's playset

Each campaign chapter in Part V offers a steading and nine playbooks. The
whole table plays in one playset; each player takes a different playbook.

## 2. Pick a playbook

Read the concepts aloud and claim what calls to you. The playbook gives you
almost everything: default stats, HP and damage die, moves, gear options,
bonds, and your community tie.

## 3. Set your stats

Every playbook distributes the array **{4, 3, 3, 2, 2, 1}** across STR, DEX,
CON, INT, WIS, CHA in a default arrangement that suits the archetype. Take
the default, or **swap any two values** — up to two swaps. One rule binds
you: your playbook's **anchor stat** (the one its signature move rolls) has
a printed minimum, and no swap may take it below that.

## 4. Name yourself and choose your Look

Pick or invent a name from the playbook's list, and choose one option from
each Look line (bearing, dress, eyes, and so on). Say what the others see.

## 5. Note your derived numbers

Copy from the playbook: **maximum HP** (base + CON), **damage die**, and
**Load** (base + STR). Start at full HP, 0 XP.

## 6. Take your starting moves

You start with your **signature** move, every **fixed** move, and the
playbook's stated number of **choice** moves (usually two — the sheet marks
how many). Check them off.

## 7. Pick your gear

The playbook offers a short list of gear choices — "choose one of…" lines.
Pick, and note anything with a special rule. Keep total weight within Load.

## 8. Write your bonds

The playbook gives fill-in-the-blank bond statements. Once everyone has
introduced their character, fill in at least two with other characters'
names. Read them aloud — bonds are promises about how your stories tangle.

## 9. Claim your community tie

Choose one option from the playbook's community-tie list. It names a person,
place, or obligation in the steading that is *yours*. This is the GM's
standing invitation to make home matter to you.

## 10. Set your embedment tracks

If your playbook carries power-structure tracks (Bond, Debt, Heat, Devotion,
Favor, Wrath, Standing, or a personal track), set each pip to its printed
starting value. The campaign chapters explain what each track means.

## 11. Introduce yourselves

Go around the table: name, Look, duty, and your community tie. The GM will
ask questions. Answer them — every answer becomes true.

# 8. Moves

A **move** is a package of rules with a trigger written in plain language:
"*When you shape arcane power while sparing the living land…*", "*When you
approach a beast calmly and show no fear…*". Moves come in two mechanical
forms.

## Rollable moves

A rollable move names a stat, a default difficulty, and a results block. To
use one:

1. **Trigger it in the fiction.** Describe your character doing the thing.
   The table agrees the move fires. (You can't invoke the move's name as an
   action — the description comes first.)
2. **Build the pool** (chapter 4). The move's printed difficulty is the
   default; the GM adjusts it for the situation, then adds boosts and
   setbacks from the fiction. On the web sheet, the move's **Roll** button
   does all of this — set difficulty, add blue/black dice, click.
3. **Read the roll against the move's results block** (chapter 5):
   - the **success** line — what you get;
   - the **failure** line — what goes wrong instead;
   - the **▲ menu** — options to buy with net advantage. This is where the
     move's hold currency is earned, if it has one ("Hold 1 String…");
   - the **▼ menu** — costs the GM picks from with net threat;
   - the **◆ line** and **✶ line** — what a triumph or despair means here.
4. **Say what it looks like.** The fiction changes; play continues.

If the dice give you something the menus don't cover, fall back to the
exchange rates in chapter 5.

## Common action moves

Most playbook moves are written on your sheet, but the following procedures
come up at every table.

**Risky check.** When danger looms, the stakes are high, and no named move
fits, roll the stat that matches your approach (chapter 4). On success, you
do it. On threat, the GM gives a lesser success, cost, consequence, or hard
choice. On failure, mark XP and the GM makes a move.

**Clash.** When you fight in melee or close quarters, roll with the stat that
fits the weapon and approach, usually STR. On success, deal your damage. On
advantage, avoid, prevent, or counter the enemy's attack, or strike hard for
an extra d6 damage while still suffering their attack. On threat, you suffer
the enemy's attack or another immediate cost. On failure, the enemy has the
upper hand.

**Let fly.** When you take an easy shot with a ranged weapon and the fiction
gives you a clear line, deal your damage. If the shot is tricky or you are
under pressure, roll DEX. On success, deal your damage. On threat, choose or
accept a cost: deplete ammo, wait for a clear shot, move into danger for the
shot, or rush it and accept the GM's complication.

**Defend.** When you take up a defensive stance or throw yourself between a
threat and your ward, roll CON. On advantage, hold Readiness; a shield gives
more. Spend Readiness one-for-one to suffer an attack's damage or effects
instead of your ward, halve an attack's effect or damage, draw attention to
yourself, or strike back with disadvantage on your damage roll. When you stop
defending or the threat passes, lose unspent Readiness.

## Static moves

A move with plain text and no stat doesn't roll — it is a standing
permission, truth, or price. When its trigger happens, do what it says. Many
static moves trade a track mark for an effect ("add 1 Debt and the patron's
door opens") — that trade *is* the move. Others simply make something always
true for you that isn't true for anyone else.

## The three kinds on your sheet

- **Signature** — your archetype's defining move, always on, usually
  carrying a named hold (chapter 9).
- **Fixed** — always on; you have all of them from the start.
- **Choice** — you have the ones you've checked; advancement adds more.

## Helping and hindering

**Aid:** if your bond, position, or skill makes the help credible, describe
how you help and give the roller **one blue die**. You are in the scene now
— the GM may spend threat from that roll against *you*.

**Interfere:** describe how you get in someone's way and impose **one black
die** on their roll — and accept the fictional consequences of being in the
way.

Both apply to other player characters and to NPCs alike. Only one aid die
and one interference die per source, and the fiction must carry it.

# 9. Currencies

Beyond the dice themselves, four kinds of resource move around the table.

## Blue and black dice

The small change of the game, granted from the fiction (chapter 4):

- The right tool, high ground, surprise, a true insight, an ally's aid →
  **+1 blue** on the relevant roll.
- Poor light, no footing, improvised gear, being watched, interference →
  **+1 black**.
- **Lasting states** persist: while a ward stands, while the barricade
  holds, the blue (or black) applies to every relevant roll until the
  fiction changes.

## Conditions

A condition is a lasting state of your body or mind: **wounded and
untreated**, **exhausted**, **shaken**, and the like. Each condition names a
pair of stats and adds **one black die to every roll using either stat**
until the condition is treated. Write conditions next to HP; erase them when
care, rest, or the fiction removes them.

## Holds

A **hold** is stored agency with a name: **Strings, Momentum, Favor, Writ,
Focus, Quarry, Reverie, Bedrock**, and others. Each hold belongs to one move.

- **Earn** hold mostly by spending net ▲ on that move's menu ("Hold 1
  String"), or 3+ net ▲ anywhere the move applies (chapter 5).
- **Spend** hold one-for-one, at any fitting moment, for the effects the
  move lists. No roll needed — spending hold is cashing a check the dice
  already wrote.
- Track holds on the sheet's counters. Unless a move says otherwise, holds
  keep until spent.

## Story Points

A shared pool of two tokens (or one per player at bigger tables), split
between the players' side and the GM's side. **Any player may spend a
player-side token; the GM spends GM-side tokens.** Spend one to:

- **Upgrade a die.** Turn one of your greens into a yellow (the GM: one
  purple into a red). The rails still bind — if the pool already holds 2
  yellows (or 2 reds), the spend adds a blue (black) die instead.
- **Declare a detail.** Add one small, true thing to the scene: there's a
  back stair; the guard is someone you know; you *did* pack it. The GM has
  final say on what's small enough.

**A spent token moves to the other side.** Every point you use arms the GM,
and every point the GM uses comes back to you. Spend at dramatic moments —
this economy is the game's pacing valve, not a routine bonus.

## Supplies run out on threat

Ammunition, torches, rations, and remedies have no tick-boxes. They deplete
when the GM (or you) spends a minor ▼ as "lose a consumable." Keep your
gear list honest and the dice will handle scarcity for you.

# 10. Harm and Recovery

Harm stays simple and does **not** use the narrative dice.

## Taking harm

When the fiction says you're hurt — a blade lands, the wall collapses, the
poison bites — the GM rolls the attacker's **damage die** (or names a fixed
amount for hazards) and you take that off your **HP**. Armor works in the
fiction: worn armor reduces incoming damage as its description says, and
sometimes turns a wound into a bruise entirely.

HP is fighting spirit, luck, grit, and plot armor as much as meat. Losing HP
does not always mean a visible wound, and a visible wound can matter even
after HP comes back.

Armor subtracts from incoming damage. Armor defaults to 0, and armor ratings
do not stack unless a piece of gear explicitly says it grants **+1 armor**.
Armor does not help against attacks that **ignore armor** or against harm
that bypasses physical defense, such as fire, poison, psychic force, or a
curse.

## Dealing harm

When the fiction says you hurt something, roll **your damage die** (printed
on your sheet) and apply it. Moves and fictional position — a blade at the
throat, a fall arranged onto stone — can raise, lower, or bypass the roll
entirely; the move's text and the GM's judgment govern.

Your damage die comes from your playbook, not your weapon. A Heavy is
dangerous even with a knife; a scholar does not become a Heavy by picking up
an axe. Do not add STR or DEX to damage rolls.

Weapons still matter because they decide what is possible. Range tags such
as **hand**, **close**, **reach**, **near**, and **far** say where the weapon
can matter. A spear can keep a knife-fighter out; a bow needs distance and a
line of fire; an ordinary blade may do nothing to a thing made of stone.

Some gear changes damage:

- **+1 damage** adds to your playbook damage roll.
- **n piercing** ignores n points of armor.
- **x piercing** ignores armor equal to the steading's current Prosperity.
- **forceful** can shove, stagger, or knock someone down.
- **messy** can maim, shatter shields, break armor, or leave a problematic
  wound when the fiction supports it.

If a move gives advantage or disadvantage on a damage roll, roll the main
damage die twice and keep the higher or lower result, then add flat bonuses.

## At zero

Hitting **0 HP** means you are out of the action. If the source could kill
you, roll **Death's Door**.

Death's Door is a flat risky check: build it as a stat 2, untrained, average
difficulty roll. On success with advantage or triumph, you wrest yourself
back to the living at **1 HP** and say how death marked you. On success with
threat or no net side effect, you are no longer dying but remain out of the
action, usually unconscious. On failure, your time has come: make one last
move as if everything broke your way, return changed as a revenant or ghost,
or call on one of the Things Below and accept what answers.

In the original Stonetop move, that same outcome is: on **10+**, return at
**1 HP** and say how death marked you; on **7-9**, you are no longer dying
but unconscious or out of action; on **6-**, choose final death, return as a
revenant or ghost, or make a bargain with the Things Below.

## Conditions

Serious harm often leaves a **condition** (chapter 9) even after HP is
restored: *wounded and untreated*, *exhausted*, *shaken*. Each adds a black
die to rolls with its paired stats until treated.

The original Stonetop debilities map cleanly to conditions:

- **Weakened:** black die on STR and DEX rolls.
- **Dazed:** black die on INT and WIS rolls.
- **Miserable:** black die on CON and CHA rolls.

## Recovery

Fiction first, as always:

- **Recover:** when you take time to catch your breath and tend what ails
  you, spend **1 use of supplies** and recover HP equal to **4 + the
  steading's Prosperity**. You cannot gain this benefit again until you take
  more damage.
- **Treating a condition or problematic wound:** say how you tend it. The GM
  says whether that is enough or what else it requires: rest, herbs, field
  surgery, a healer, a roll, or a facility.
- **Make camp:** when you settle in to rest in an unsafe area, each member
  consumes **1 use of supplies or provisions**. If you eat and sleep, choose
  one: recover HP equal to **half your maximum HP, rounded up**, or clear a
  condition. For original Stonetop playbooks, this is the choice to clear a
  debility. If the rest was peaceful and comfortable, take a blue die on your
  next roll. If you use a bedroll, recover **+1d6 HP**.
- **Real care at home** — a healer's hands, an infirmary bed, days of rest
  at the steading — can restore HP fully and clear conditions when the
  fiction gives it time and safety.
- **Some things don't heal on rest**: personal tracks like Defilement or
  Corruption (chapter 14) never fade on their own, and a god's Wrath
  (chapter 13) must be appeased, not slept off.

# 11. Growing

## Marking XP

Mark 1 XP:

- **Every time a roll fails.** No exceptions unless a move says so.
- **When a move tells you to.**
- **At the end of each session**, for each of these the table agrees you
  did: resolved a bond (then rewrite it), and honored your duty line at real
  cost.

## Spending XP

When you have **5 XP**, erase them and take **one checkbox from your
advancement list**. Advancements are playbook-specific: new choice moves, a
second training upgrade on a move you have (raising it to two yellow dice —
the cap), improvements to holds, followers, gear, or standing.

HP does not automatically increase when you spend XP or take an advancement.
Your maximum HP changes only if a playbook move or advancement explicitly
says it does.

## Advanced advancements

Each playbook's **Advanced** list unlocks after several basic picks (the
sheet marks the requirement). Only there can you **raise a stat by 1** (to
the cap of 5). Stat raises are meant to feel like turning points in the
character's story, not shopping — expect the GM to ask what changed.

## Growth beyond the sheet

Not everything you gain is a checkbox. Standing with a patron, a god's
favor, the steading's regard, a hard-won ally, a new facility at home —
Parts III and IV cover the tracks and rolls those live on. A character a
year into play is bigger than their advancement column shows.

# 12. Patrons

*This chapter applies in campaigns whose playbooks carry patron tracks. If
your sheet has no Bond/Debt/Heat rows, skip it.*

A **patron** is a powerful backer: a noble house, thieves' guild, merchant
cartel, temple, hidden master, wizard, crime lord, military order, ancient
spirit, or distant empire. A patron makes power transactional — they give
access, protection, favors, secrets, magic, work, and trouble.

Your relationship with a patron lives on three tracks on your sheet's
embedment rail.

## Patron types

Use these as templates when you create or choose a patron.

| Type | Gifts | Prices |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Noble House | Status, soldiers, legal reach | Reputation, obedience, marriage, taxes |
| Thieves' Guild | Fences, spies, safehouses | Cuts, silence, underworld loyalty |
| Temple | Healing, sanctuary, relic access | Tithes, doctrine, public service |
| Arcane Master | Spells, lore, strange tools | Experiments, secrets, corruption |
| Merchant League | Credit, cargo, maps | Contracts, monopolies, debt |
| Warlord | Troops, weapons, intimidation | Violence, conquest, tribute |
| Ancient Spirit | Omens, land magic, protection | Rites, taboos, sacrifice |
| Outsider | Impossible aid, curses, visions | Soul marks, bargains, future claims |

## Bond (0–3) — how much they trust you

- **0** — Known contact. The patron recognizes you.
- **1** — Useful asset. They'll spend minor resources on you.
- **2** — Trusted agent. Serious aid, sensitive work.
- **3** — Marked favorite. Your success is part of their power.

**Raise Bond** by completing a patron's demand, protecting their interests
at real cost, bringing them rare treasure, or expanding their influence.
**Lower Bond** by refusing a demand, embarrassing them, aiding a rival,
breaking their taboo, or failing in a way that damages their agenda.

## Debt (0–6) — what they can call in

**Add 1 Debt** when you: ask for help without paying now · invoke the patron
while in danger · use their name to gain access · fail a patron move · keep
a gift after refusing its price.

**Clear Debt** when you: complete a demand · pay a costly tithe, secret,
service, hostage, oath, or treasure · accept lasting fallout on the patron's
behalf.

**At Debt 6** the patron makes a major demand immediately. Once you accept
or resolve it, Debt resets to 3.

## Heat (0–4) — who's watching

Attention from rivals, authorities, enemies, and suspicious locals.

**0** quiet · **1** rumors · **2** watched · **3** targeted · **4** open
retaliation.

Heat rises when patron business becomes public, when you act violently or
dramatically, or when rivals are provoked. It falls through time, bribes,
diplomacy, scapegoats, or decisive victories.

## The move: Invoke Patron

*When you ask a patron for immediate aid*, say what you want and what
leverage you have. Build the pool:

- **Positive:** green dice equal to your **Bond**. +1 blue if you have a
  relevant asset or connection. You may always **add 1 Debt to upgrade one
  green to yellow**.
- **Negative:** difficulty by the request — modest **average (2)**,
  dangerous **hard (3)**, anything that could expose or weaken the patron
  **daunting (4)**. **+1 black die per point of your current Heat.**

**Success with ▲ — choose two:**

- The aid arrives quickly.
- The aid is exactly what you asked for.
- No new Debt.
- Heat does not rise.
- The patron reveals useful extra information.

**Clean success — choose one** from the list above, and **one cost** from
the list below.

**Success with ▼ — choose one** from the list above; the GM also chooses
one:

- Add 1 Debt.
- Add 1 Heat.
- The aid is partial, delayed, or compromised.
- The patron attaches a demand.
- A rival learns about the request.

**Failure:** the patron may still answer if it's interesting, but things
turn: add 1 Debt *and* 1 Heat, or the patron is offended.

## What patrons give

Shelter · legal cover · forged papers · rumors · safe passage · a specialist
hireling · a squad of rough allies · a minor magic item · a curse lifted for
a while · a door opened in high society · a rival distracted · a secret
revealed · support for the steading.

## What patrons ask

When Debt comes due, expect work: recover a stolen object, silence a
witness, protect a shipment, spy on a rival, escort a dangerous guest, break
a taboo to prove loyalty, deliver tribute, endorse the patron publicly,
sabotage a rival's works, hide someone hunted, enter a ruin for something
unnamed, or accept a visible mark of service. Saying no is allowed — and
costs Bond.

# 13. Gods

*This chapter applies in campaigns whose playbooks carry divine tracks. If
your sheet has no Devotion/Favor/Wrath rows, skip it.*

A **god** is a power of worship, fear, place, and portfolio: a true deity,
an ancestor host, a saint, a demon-god, a river's spirit, a dead empire's
idol, a city cult. Gods are more public than patrons and more dangerous to
treat casually. A god's **portfolio** — storms and sea, hearth and oaths,
secrets and thieves, war, mercy, fate — defines what it can easily touch.

## Everyday religion

Most people honor many gods: a farmer prays to rain, sun, hearth, roads,
birth, and death in the same month. Exclusive devotion is for priests,
zealots, chosen heroes, and the desperate. This matters at the table:

- Anyone can make offerings and receive omens.
- Breaking a *local* god's taboo can hurt you even if you follow another.
- Rival gods can both hold legitimate claims on you.

## Devotion (0–3) — your standing

- **0** — occasional worshipper · **1** — sworn faithful · **2** — favored
  servant · **3** — chosen instrument.

**Gain** it by upholding a tenet at cost, completing a holy task, making a
meaningful offering, protecting the god's people, restoring a shrine, or
defeating a rival power. **Lose** it by breaking a taboo, profaning a holy
place, ignoring a direct omen, aiding a hated rival, or using divine gifts
selfishly.

## Favor (0–3) — spendable grace

Favor is a hold (chapter 9) granted by the god. You can carry at most 3
from one god.

**Gain 1 Favor** when you complete a rite, sacrifice treasure, fulfill a
vow, or roll success with ▲ on a prayer.

**Spend 1 Favor** to:

- Add 1 blue die to a check that clearly serves the god's portfolio.
- Ask the GM for an omen.
- Turn success-with-▼ on a holy act into success-with-▲.
- Protect an ally from a minor curse, fear, or spiritual harm.
- Declare a small religious contact, shrine, or sign exists nearby.

## The move: Pray for Aid

*When you pray for divine aid under pressure*, name the god, state the
request, and offer something meaningful. Build the pool:

- **Positive:** green dice equal to your **Devotion**. +1 blue for a
  relevant offering or holy relic. You may **spend 1 Favor to upgrade one
  green to yellow**.
- **Negative:** difficulty set by the GM — usually **average (2)** or
  **hard (3)**.

**Success with ▲ — the god intervenes clearly. Choose one:**

- Healing or relief from a condition.
- Protection from a specific danger.
- Revelation of a hidden truth.
- A weapon, tool, or spell empowered for one scene.
- A hostile spirit, undead, curse, or demon forced back.
- A path opened where none seemed available.

**Success with ▼ — the aid comes with a condition. Choose one:**

- Mark 1 Favor owed as a vow.
- The effect is brief or incomplete.
- The god demands visible obedience, now.
- A rival power notices.
- The answer is symbolic and must be interpreted.

**Failure:** the god is silent, offended, or terrifyingly present. Things
turn — and Wrath may rise.

## Wrath (0–4) — divine displeasure

- **0** none · **1** bad omens · **2** blessings fail or turn unreliable ·
  **3** the god sends agents, dreams, curses, or monsters · **4** you are
  excommunicated, cursed, hunted, or spiritually marked.

Reduce Wrath through confession, restitution, pilgrimage, sacrifice,
completing a hard command, or making peace with the offended power. Wrath
never fades on its own.

## Offerings

An offering should cost something.

| Offering | Usual effect |
| --- | --- |
| Coin, gems, or treasure | Gain Favor, if the amount matters to you |
| Food, wine, incense, candles | Seek an omen or minor blessing |
| Blood, pain, fasting, ordeal | Ask protection, cleansing, or power |
| A vow, oath, or public service | Gain Devotion when fulfilled |
| A relic, enemy trophy, or reclaimed shrine | Reduce Wrath or raise Devotion |

# 14. Personal Tracks

Some playbooks carry a track that measures not a relationship but a
transformation: **Defilement** on the Defiler, **Corruption** on the Dabbler
in the Black Arts, **Scars** on the Preserver, and their kin. These are the
marks the character's own power leaves on them.

Three rules set personal tracks apart from every other number on your sheet:

1. **They do not heal.** No rest, no infirmary, no season at home lowers a
   personal track. If a way back exists at all, it is a quest, a price, or a
   miracle — the campaign chapter or the GM will say.
2. **They rise when you choose.** Almost every mark on a personal track
   comes from a choice the move offered you: take the power now and mark, or
   go without. The track is a record of what you decided you were willing to
   become.
3. **The top is a threshold, not a death.** When a personal track fills, the
   GM tells you what you have become — and what that means is different in
   every campaign. The character may pass out of your hands, change sides,
   change nature, or carry on transformed. Read your playbook's track note;
   it states the threshold rule.

Treat a personal track as story, not tax. Every pip should be a scene: the
day the ground died at your touch, the night the book whispered back.

# 15. Your Steading

The party's home — the **steading** — is a shared character with its own
sheet. A steading might be a village, a war-camp, a tribe's holt, a cellar
den, a tower, a ship. Its sheet carries what the place *is* and what it can
*do*.

## The steading sheet

- **Ratings**, each from −1 to +3:
  - **Scale** — size, reach, and population.
  - **Safety** — walls, guards, secrecy, law, defensibility.
  - **Prosperity** — money, trade, harvest, craft, comfort.
  - **Influence** — reputation, political reach, faith, fear, alliances.
  - **Supply** — stores, tools, food, medicine, repair capacity.
- **Facilities** — the working parts of the place (a forge, an infirmary, a
  shrine, a hidden room…). Each names its function, which rating it uses,
  one order it performs especially well, and one vulnerability.
- **Named people** — the NPCs your community ties point at.
- **Trouble (0–4)** — pressure on the steading: **0** stable · **1**
  complaints, shortages, rumors · **2** open problem · **3** crisis · **4**
  disaster. At Trouble 4 the crisis breaks and reshapes the situation;
  afterward Trouble resets to 2.
- **Fortunes (1–5)** — the steading's confidence and goodwill, rolled as a
  pool on season moves and steading turns, exactly like a stat.
- **Surplus** — the steading's hold currency: stores put by. Spend it to
  feed the winter, fund projects, or trade. **Surplus 0 in winter is a
  crisis.**

Some steadings also hold a **strange resource** — a rare, dangerous asset
(true steel, old magic, a secret way). Its entry says what it grants and
what it draws.

When the steading exploits a strange resource, add **+1 Prosperity** while
the resource flows. If the steading fails a **Gather**, **Craft**, **Trade**,
or **Build** order while exploiting it, the GM chooses or rolls a side
effect:

| d10 | Resource side effect |
| ---: | --- |
| 1 | Workers develop marks, mutations, dreams, or compulsions. |
| 2 | A faction demands exclusive rights. |
| 3 | A god, spirit, or monster claims the resource as sacred. |
| 4 | The resource changes a facility. |
| 5 | Local animals, crops, or weather become strange. |
| 6 | A cure, stabilizer, or protective charm becomes necessary. |
| 7 | Smugglers and raiders arrive. |
| 8 | The resource empowers enemies as easily as allies. |
| 9 | The supply is richer but more dangerous than expected. |
| 10 | The resource awakens something below. |

## The season move

Once each season, the steading rolls for how the season goes:

- **Positive:** green dice equal to **Fortunes**, upgraded once if a
  facility clearly leads the season's work. Season rolls are **always
  risky** — the year always carries a yellow and a red.
- **Negative:** difficulty set by the season — spring **average**, deep
  winter **hard** or worse.

Read it on the grid as ever: ▲ can be spent for **Surplus**; ▼ ticks
**Trouble**; **◆** is a windfall — an alliance, a birth, a discovery; **✶**
is disaster — raid, fire, plague.

## Steading turns

When a week of downtime passes, after an expedition, or when the GM says
the wider world has moved, the steading may take a **turn**:

1. **Choose one order** from the table below.
2. **Choose one facility to lead it.**
3. **Build the pool:** greens equal to the rating the order uses (if the
   rating is negative, roll 0 greens and add 1 black die). Default
   difficulty **average (2)**.
4. **Roll and apply.** Success with ▲: clean, plus one extra benefit.
   Success with ▼: it works, but choose one cost. Failure: it fails or
   succeeds at a serious price.
5. The world moves too — expect one external pressure to advance.

| Order | Rating | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Build | Supply | Add, repair, or upgrade a facility |
| Craft | Prosperity | Produce gear, tools, medicine, or trade goods |
| Gather | Supply | Stock food, water, fuel, herbs, ore |
| Recruit | Influence | Attract hirelings, guards, specialists, settlers |
| Research | Influence or Prosperity | Learn lore, maps, weaknesses, rituals |
| Trade | Prosperity | Convert goods into coin, favors, rare supplies |
| Patrol | Safety | Reduce danger, scout threats, protect roads |
| Hide | Safety | Conceal people, treasure, fugitives, forbidden work |
| Heal | Supply | Recover wounds, conditions, disease, morale |
| Celebrate | Influence | Reduce unrest, gain rumors, create bonds |
| Petition | Influence | Ask a patron, god, court, or faction for support |

**Extra benefits** (success with ▲ — choose one): gain 1 Surplus · gain 1
Coin · gain 1 Favor with a patron, god, or faction · reduce Heat, Trouble,
or Wrath by 1 · finish faster than expected · a hireling or ally improves ·
learn a useful secret · create an opening for the next adventure.

**Costs** (success with ▼ — choose one): spend 1 Surplus · spend 1 Coin ·
add 1 Trouble · add 1 Heat · a facility is strained and can't lead the next
turn · a hireling is injured, offended, missing, or compromised · a patron
demands credit or payment · a rival copies, blocks, or exploits the work.

## Personal downtime

When the party has time between expeditions, each player can choose one
downtime option before the next expedition, season move, or steading turn
pushes play forward:

| Option | Result |
| --- | --- |
| Rest | Recover HP or make space for longer healing. |
| Treat a condition | Tend a condition or problematic wound; the GM says what care is enough. |
| Repair gear | Fix damaged armor, tools, weapons, or expedition equipment. |
| Craft something | Make a useful item if you have materials, tools, and time. |
| Train or practice | Work toward an advancement, learn a technique, or justify new training. |
| Gather rumors | Ask what people are saying and what threat is moving. |
| Make offerings | Appease a god, spirit, ancestor, or local power. |
| Visit a patron | Ask for work, aid, forgiveness, or terms. |
| Pay down Debt | Satisfy a patron's price, service, tithe, or demand. |
| Reduce Heat | Lie low, bribe, apologize, make amends, or redirect attention. |
| Work on a steading order | Help a facility, project, or order directly. |
| Strengthen a bond | Spend meaningful time with another PC or an NPC tie. |
| Recruit help | Look for hirelings, guards, guides, specialists, or followers. |
| Research a threat | Study lore, maps, weaknesses, rituals, or old stories. |
| Prepare for an expedition | Secure supplies, maps, transport, tools, medicine, or introductions. |

## The steading and the party

The steading creates adventures; it doesn't replace them. If home can solve
a problem alone, let it. When it can't, the turn's result reveals what the
characters must do in person — and that's next session. Your **Standing
(0–3)** with the steading, where your campaign uses it, measures how hard
the community will rally for *you* in particular.

# 16. Expeditions

An **expedition** is the shape most adventures take: the party leaves the
steading with a purpose, faces what's out there, and comes home changed —
to a home that has also changed.

## Why you go

Expeditions come in three broad kinds, and a good season mixes them:

- **Neutralizing threats.** Something out there endangers home — raiders
  massing, a beast in the hills, a corruption spreading — and waiting will
  make it worse.
- **Securing opportunities.** Home needs something it cannot make: rare
  materials, a trade route, an alliance, knowledge buried in a ruin.
- **Personal drives.** Your duty line, your bonds, your community tie, your
  patron's demand, your god's command — the pulls that are yours alone.

When the table isn't sure what to do next, look at the steading sheet: its
Trouble, its pressures, and its needs are a menu of expeditions.

## Preparing

Preparation is fiction, and it counts. Before you leave:

- **Provision.** Say what you carry, within Load. Supplies fail on threat
  spends (chapter 9), so what you packed matters exactly as long as you
  packed it.
- **Ask around.** Rumors, maps, and elders' warnings are how the GM tells
  you the difficulty in advance.
- **Set affairs at home.** An expedition usually spans a steading turn —
  choose the order home takes while you're gone, because the world moves
  whether you're there or not.

## Out there

On the road and in the ruin, the ordinary rules carry everything: the
conversation, moves, checks, harm. Two habits serve every party:

- **Spend advantage forward.** Blue dice handed to the next scout, hold
  banked for the crisis — expeditions reward the party that treats ▲ as a
  shared resource.
- **Respect despair.** One risky roll in twelve turns memorably wrong. Deep
  in a ruin, that statistic is a planning tool: keep a way out, keep a
  reserve, keep the wounded moving.

## Coming home

Homecoming closes the loop. Tell the steading what happened — some returns
raise Fortunes or Surplus, some tick Trouble or Heat, and the GM will say
which. Take your steading turn if a week passes. Treat wounds, clear
conditions, honor what you promised, and mark end-of-session XP. Then look
at what the season roll and the pressures have set in motion, and choose
where you're needed next.

# 17. Stonetop

## The premise

A small farming village stands alone at the edge of the known world, under a
great standing stone, a hard week from anywhere. Your characters are not
visitors — they are the village's own: its keeper of old rites, its sheriff,
its huntress, its restless youth. The wilds around are old and haunted, the
neighbors are far and not all friendly, and every year the village either
grows a little stronger or slips a little closer to the dark. This is hearth
fantasy: the adventure matters because home does.

*A note on this chapter.* Stonetop's nine playbooks are drawn from a
published game that this campaign converts for table use. Out of respect for
that work, this chapter does not reproduce the playbooks' text. What follows
are **conversion profiles**: everything you need to run each archetype on
this engine — its numbers, its shape, and how its moves translate — written
in this book's own words. Play with the converted sheets (the web sheets
carry the full detail), and with a copy of the original game on the shelf it
came from.

## Your home

The village is the campaign's steading (chapter 15). Set it up with the GM
at the table: its ratings are those of a small, remote, self-reliant farming
settlement — modest Scale, decent Supply, low Influence — with facilities
along the lines of a shrine to the local goddess, a common hall, a smithy,
and a palisade. Its Fortunes carry the year; its Surplus feeds the winter;
its Trouble is never far. The old roads, the ruins beneath and beyond, and
the village's two distant neighbor-settlements — a marsh trade-town and a
mining camp — supply a steady stream of expeditions of all three kinds
(chapter 16).

## Embedment style

Full suite. Stonetop playbooks use the village's gods (chapter 13 — the
local earth-mother faith and the shrine-keeping that goes with it), patrons
where the fiction offers them (chapter 12), Standing with the village, and
bonds and community ties throughout. The steading is the campaign's heart:
expect season moves and steading turns every few sessions.

## Converting the playbooks

Every move on the original playbooks converts by the standard map, which the
GM applies on the fly wherever a sheet hasn't already been converted:

- "Roll plus a stat" → build a pool on that stat (chapter 4), trained if
  you have the move. Strong hit → **success with ▲**; weak hit → **success
  with ▼** (or clean); miss → **failure, mark XP**.
- "Take +1 forward / ongoing" → **+1 blue die** on that roll / while the
  fiction holds. Disadvantage → **+1 black die**.
- "Hold 1 / spend hold" → identical here (chapter 9), earned via ▲ menus.
- Uses, charges, and stock-pouches → a named **hold** refilled by rite,
  forage, or rest as the move describes.
- ◆ and ✶ have no old equivalent: on ◆, read the move's most generous
  line and then some; on ✶, its worst outcome arrives immediately.

## Original rules values to preserve

The following Stonetop values are not optional color; they are the numbers
the conversion keeps.

# 18. The Free Legion

## The premise

Under a dying sun, on a world of ash, salt, and bone, an army of escaped
gladiator-slaves is on the move. The city's heralds call them "the broken
chain." They call themselves the Free Legion — a straggle of mismatched arms,
sun-bleached tents, and yoked pack-beasts, ringed each dusk by a
thorn-and-spear stockade, never sleeping twice in the same dry wash. Your
characters broke the pens or joined the column after. Now comes the hard
part: surviving the pursuit, holding the freed together, and finding
something worth calling home.

This is a world where metal is treasure, water is politics, and magic has a
price the land itself pays. Wizard-tyrants rule the cities through templar
bureaucracies; the faiths that answer prayers belong to the raw elements;
and every fire lit by sorcery leaves the ground a little more dead — unless
the caster is one of the rare few who take gently.

## Your home

The steading is the Legion itself: a **mobile war-camp** (Scale +1, Safety
−1, Prosperity 0, Influence +1, Supply 0), with two facilities on the move —
the **Forge-Wagon**, which turns broken loot into usable weapons overnight
but goes cold without smith or fuel, and the **Field-Infirmary**, which
keeps the badly hurt alive through one more march but collapses fast when
water runs short.

The Legion guards one strange resource: the **Iron Coffer**, a locked chest
of true sword-steel in a land of bone and obsidian — a few dozen blades'
worth, no more. It arms the Legion's best, and it draws every raider,
merchant house, and templar who hears the rumor.

People your community ties point at: **Karth One-Hand**, the war-leader who
struck the first blow in the pens, tired and harder than he lets on;
**Senna**, the quartermaster who actually keeps the Legion alive and resents
that no one thanks her; **Little Vurt**, a freed child the whole camp has
silently agreed to protect; and **the Veiled One**, a mage the Legion needs
and fears — because no one is sure whether the magic is the gentle kind or
the kind that kills the ground.

What the Legion needs cannot be bought: legitimacy and water security — a
place and a claim that make it a people the world must reckon with, not a
mob to be hunted down.

## Embedment style

**Power-structure, patron-heavy.** Most playbooks here answer to someone:
a hidden alliance of gentle mages, a merchant house, a sorcerer-king's
bureaucracy, a performers' guild, an elf raiding tribe (chapter 12). The
Elemental Cleric serves an element as a god (chapter 13). The Defiler and
the Preserver carry personal tracks — **Defilement** and **Scars** — that
never heal (chapter 14). The Gladiator and the Legion's own are embedded in
the steading itself.

# 19. The Hollow-Oak Holt

## The premise

Deep in an old forest, sheltered in a fold of hills, a small tribe of elves
lives in and around a single enormous, ancient hollow oak — dens and
platforms grown and woven into the great tree, wolf-trails threading out to
the hunting grounds, smoke curling from the Mother Tree's heart. The world
is hardening: each winter runs longer, humans push at the wood's edge, and
the game grows thin. Of late the tribe has taken in an outsider or two.

The magic here is nobody's loan. It is in the blood and the land — shapers
who coax living wood and stone, healers who mend flesh with their hands,
senders who speak mind to mind — and each generation shapes a little less,
remembers a little less, while the cold presses a little closer.

## Your home

The steading is the **Holt** (Scale 0, Safety +1, Prosperity 0, Influence
−1, Supply +1), with two facilities: the **Mother Tree**, shelter and heart
of the tribe, which hides and warms everyone through the worst weather but
cannot forgive fire; and the **Shaping-Grove**, where the shapers work wood,
stone, and flesh — the tribe's craft, healing, and quiet magic, resting on
a very few gifted hands.

The Holt's strange resource is the **old magic in the land** itself: it
cannot be mined or hoarded, only lived with and tended — and it draws the
notice of humans (as devilry) and of older things that remember when the
magic was stronger.

## Embedment style

**Village.** No patron, god, or bastion tracks — no pips at all on the
embedment rail. The bonds *are* the embedment: recognition, sending, pack
and tribe all live in the fill-in statements between characters and in each
playbook's community tie to the Holt. Chapters 12–14 are not used in this
campaign; the steading rules (chapter 15) very much are.

# 20. The Sign of the Black Cat

## The premise

In the poorest, oldest quarter of a great, fog-choked, god-ridden city —
where the fog never fully lifts and the watch never fully enters — a band
of ne'er-do-wells works out of a rented cellar under a failing wine-shop.
The faded sign above gives the band its name. The room below is
smoke-stained, the chairs are mismatched, the door is good and strong, and
there is a bolt-hole behind the wine-casks and a black cat no one admits to
feeding.

The city runs on guilds, and the guilds mean it: thieving without a license
is a hanging matter twice over — once by the law, once by the Thieves'
Guild. A hundred small gods jostle for worshippers along a single crowded
street, and some of them answer. The band's ambitions are simple: stay fed,
stay unhanged, and pull off the one score that buys a better life — or at
least a better cellar.

## Your home

The steading is the **cellar-den** (Scale −1, Safety +1, Prosperity 0,
Influence +1, Supply 0), with two facilities: the **Cellar-Den** itself,
which makes a person or a thing disappear for a while but is one betrayed
door away from a trap with no way out; and the **Fence's Corner**, which
moves hot goods quietly — though every sale leaves a thread the Thieves'
Guild can pull.

Beneath the floor lies the den's secret and strange resource: the
**Underway**, a forgotten branch of the city's ancient drains and
smugglers' tunnels — a way under half the quarter, known to almost no one,
mapped only in one scavenger's head. What uses the Underway does not only
run one direction.

People your community ties point at: **Old Sabra**, who keeps the den, pays
the rent, and remembers every favor owed in the quarter; **Feathers**, the
guild's soft-spoken collector, friendly right up until he isn't; **Brother
Gnarl**, a gutter-priest of a small, spiteful god, right about omens just
often enough to be frightening; and **Wren**, a lookout too young for this
life, whom the whole band silently protects.

## Embedment style

**Power-structure, patron-heavy.** The guilds and brotherhoods are patrons
(chapter 12); the street's small gods take worship and give trouble
(chapter 13); the Dabbler in the Black Arts carries a **Corruption**

## Appendix E — Harm reference

**Taking harm:** subtract armor from incoming damage, then subtract the
remainder from HP. Armor defaults to 0 and does not stack unless gear says
**+1 armor**. Armor is ignored by attacks that ignore armor or bypass
physical defense.

**Dealing harm:** roll your playbook damage die. Do not add STR or DEX.
Weapons decide reach, plausibility, and tags; only special gear adds damage.

| Tag or rule | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| +1 damage | Add 1 to the playbook damage roll. |
| n piercing | Ignore n armor. |
| x piercing | Ignore armor equal to steading Prosperity. |
| forceful | Shove, stagger, knock down, or drive back when fiction supports it. |
| messy | Maim, shatter, break armor, or leave a problematic wound when fiction supports it. |
| damage advantage | Roll the damage die twice and keep the higher result, then add flat bonuses. |
| damage disadvantage | Roll the damage die twice and keep the lower result, then add flat bonuses. |

**Recovery:** Recover heals **4 + Prosperity** HP for 1 use of supplies and
cannot be used again until you take more damage. Make Camp heals **half max
HP, rounded up**, or clears a condition. A bedroll adds **+1d6 HP**.

**Death's Door:** at 0 HP from a lethal source, roll the flat risky Death's
Door check. A strong result returns you to **1 HP**; a weak result leaves you
alive but out of action; failure means death, revenant or ghost, or a bargain
with the Things Below.

## Appendix F — Glossary

- **Advantage (▲)** — positive side-effect symbol; cancels threat one for
  one; spent on menus, holds, and edges.
- **Anchor stat** — the stat a playbook's signature move rolls; swaps can't
  take it below its printed minimum.
- **Bond (track)** — a patron's trust, 0–3; also **Bonds**, the fill-in
  statements tying characters together.
- **Boost die** — blue d6, a momentary edge.
- **Challenge die** — red d12, real peril; the only die with ✶.
- **Check** — a roll of the dice pool.
- **Condition** — lasting state naming two stats; +1 black on rolls with
  either until treated.
- **Debt** — what a patron can call in, 0–6.
- **Despair (✶)** — red-die symbol; a dramatic turn against you; counts one
  failure; never cancelled.
- **Devotion** — standing with a god, 0–3.
- **Difficulty die** — purple d8; the task rolling against you.
- **Duty** — the standing pressure printed on your playbook's header.
- **Embedment** — the sheet's right rail: tracks, bonds, community tie.
- **Facility** — a working part of the steading, with a function and a
  vulnerability.
- **Favor** — spendable divine hold, max 3 per god.
- **Fiction first** — what's true in the story decides what the dice do,
  never the reverse.
- **Fortunes** — the steading's confidence, 1–5, rolled on season moves.
- **GM** — the player who runs the world.
- **Heat** — unwanted attention, 0–4.
- **Hold** — stored agency with a name (Strings, Momentum…), earned via ▲,
  spent 1:1.
- **HP** — hit points; harm comes off them; 0 is death's door.
- **Load** — carrying capacity.
- **Move** — a rule with a fictional trigger; rollable or static.
- **Playbook** — a ready-made archetype: stats, moves, gear, ties.
- **Pool** — the dice you throw: positive side vs negative side.
- **Proficiency die** — yellow d12, trained mastery; the only die with ◆.
- **Risky** — carrying real stakes; forces at least 1 yellow and 1 red.
- **Season move** — the steading's once-a-season roll on Fortunes.
- **Setback die** — black d6, a momentary hindrance.
- **Standing** — your personal credit with the steading, 0–3.
- **Steading** — the party's shared home; a character with its own sheet.
- **Steading turn** — a downtime order rolled on a rating.
- **Story Point** — shared table currency; upgrade a die or declare a
  detail; spending flips it to the other side.
- **Surplus** — the steading's stored plenty; its hold currency.
- **Threat (▼)** — negative side-effect symbol; cancels advantage.
- **Track** — a pip row on a sheet: standings and pressures, never dice.
- **Triumph (◆)** — yellow-die symbol; a remarkable turn in your favor;
  counts one success; never cancelled.
- **Trouble** — pressure on the steading, 0–4.
- **XP** — experience: marked on failures and triggers; 5 buys an
  advancement.
