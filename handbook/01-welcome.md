# 1. Welcome

This is a tabletop roleplaying game. One player, the **Game Master** (GM),
describes a world and plays everyone in it except the heroes. Every other
player takes one hero — a **character** — and says what that character does.
The game is the conversation between you: the GM describes a situation, you
respond as your character, and together you find out what happens.

Most of the time, what happens is obvious and nobody rolls anything. When
your character attempts something risky — something that could go wrong in an
interesting way — you build a pool of dice, roll it, and read the result. The
dice don't just say whether you succeeded. They also say what it cost, what
you gained on the side, and whether the situation just turned sharply in your
favor or against you.

Your character is not a wandering adventurer from nowhere. Each campaign in
this book gives the party a shared home — a village, a war-camp, a tribe's
refuge, a rented cellar — called a **steading**, with people in it your
characters know and care about. You leave home to deal with threats and chase
opportunities, and what you do out there changes life back home. The steading
has its own sheet, its own dice rolls, and its own fortunes across the
seasons.

## What you need at the table

- **One set of narrative dice** — six special dice described in chapter 2 —
  or the web character sheet, which rolls virtual ones. You will also want an
  ordinary d6, d8, and d10 for damage.
- **A playbook sheet for each player.** A playbook is a ready-made character
  archetype — the campaign chapters in Part V each offer nine. Print the A3
  sheet or use the web sheet.
- **The steading sheet** for your campaign.
- **Two tokens** for Story Points (chapter 9). Start with one on the players'
  side of the table and one on the GM's side.

## Who does what

**You** describe what your character does, concretely, in the world: not "I
use my persuade ability" but "I lean across the table and remind him whose
coin paid for this room." You roll dice when the rules say to, answer the
GM's questions about your character, and mark the sheet when things change.

**The GM** describes everything else, plays every other person and creature,
sets how difficult tasks are, and — when the dice go against you — decides
what goes wrong. The GM is not your opponent. The GM's job is to make the
world honest: dangerous when it should be, generous when you've earned it.

**The rules** decide the moments in between: whether the leap succeeds,
whether the lie holds, what the magic costs. When a rule and the ongoing
story disagree about what makes sense, the story — what everyone has agreed
is true in the world — wins. That principle is called **fiction first**, and
it is the single most important rule in this book.
