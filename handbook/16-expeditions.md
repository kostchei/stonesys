# 16. Expeditions

An **expedition** is the shape most adventures take: the party leaves the
steading with a purpose, faces what's out there, and comes home changed —
to a home that has also changed.

## Why you go

Expeditions come in three broad kinds, and a good season mixes them:

- **Neutralizing threats.** Something out there endangers home — raiders
  massing, a beast in the hills, a corruption spreading — and waiting will
  make it worse.
- **Securing opportunities.** Home needs something it cannot make: rare
  materials, a trade route, an alliance, knowledge buried in a ruin.
- **Personal drives.** Your duty line, your bonds, your community tie, your
  patron's demand, your god's command — the pulls that are yours alone.

When the table isn't sure what to do next, look at the steading sheet: its
Trouble, its pressures, and its needs are a menu of expeditions.

## Preparing

Preparation is fiction, and it counts. Before you leave:

- **Provision.** Say what you carry, within Load. Supplies fail on threat
  spends (chapter 9), so what you packed matters exactly as long as you
  packed it.
- **Ask around.** Rumors, maps, and elders' warnings are how the GM tells
  you the difficulty in advance.
- **Set affairs at home.** An expedition usually spans a steading turn —
  choose the order home takes while you're gone, because the world moves
  whether you're there or not.

## Out there

On the road and in the ruin, the ordinary rules carry everything: the
conversation, moves, checks, harm. Two habits serve every party:

- **Spend advantage forward.** Blue dice handed to the next scout, hold
  banked for the crisis — expeditions reward the party that treats ▲ as a
  shared resource.
- **Respect despair.** One risky roll in twelve turns memorably wrong. Deep
  in a ruin, that statistic is a planning tool: keep a way out, keep a
  reserve, keep the wounded moving.

## Coming home

Homecoming closes the loop. Tell the steading what happened — some returns
raise Fortunes or Surplus, some tick Trouble or Heat, and the GM will say
which. Take your steading turn if a week passes. Treat wounds, clear
conditions, honor what you promised, and mark end-of-session XP. Then look
at what the season roll and the pressures have set in motion, and choose
where you're needed next.
