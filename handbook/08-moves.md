# 8. Moves

A **move** is a package of rules with a trigger written in plain language:
"*When you shape arcane power while sparing the living land…*", "*When you
approach a beast calmly and show no fear…*". Moves come in two mechanical
forms.

## Rollable moves

A rollable move names a stat, a default difficulty, and a results block. To
use one:

1. **Trigger it in the fiction.** Describe your character doing the thing.
   The table agrees the move fires. (You can't invoke the move's name as an
   action — the description comes first.)
2. **Build the pool** (chapter 4). The move's printed difficulty is the
   default; the GM adjusts it for the situation, then adds boosts and
   setbacks from the fiction. On the web sheet, the move's **Roll** button
   does all of this — set difficulty, add blue/black dice, click.
3. **Read the roll against the move's results block** (chapter 5):
   - the **success** line — what you get;
   - the **failure** line — what goes wrong instead;
   - the **▲ menu** — options to buy with net advantage. This is where the
     move's hold currency is earned, if it has one ("Hold 1 String…");
   - the **▼ menu** — costs the GM picks from with net threat;
   - the **◆ line** and **✶ line** — what a triumph or despair means here.
4. **Say what it looks like.** The fiction changes; play continues.

If the dice give you something the menus don't cover, fall back to the
exchange rates in chapter 5.

## Static moves

A move with plain text and no stat doesn't roll — it is a standing
permission, truth, or price. When its trigger happens, do what it says. Many
static moves trade a track mark for an effect ("add 1 Debt and the patron's
door opens") — that trade *is* the move. Others simply make something always
true for you that isn't true for anyone else.

## The three kinds on your sheet

- **Signature** — your archetype's defining move, always on, usually
  carrying a named hold (chapter 9).
- **Fixed** — always on; you have all of them from the start.
- **Choice** — you have the ones you've checked; advancement adds more.

## Helping and hindering

**Aid:** if your bond, position, or skill makes the help credible, describe
how you help and give the roller **one blue die**. You are in the scene now
— the GM may spend threat from that roll against *you*.

**Interfere:** describe how you get in someone's way and impose **one black
die** on their roll — and accept the fictional consequences of being in the
way.

Both apply to other player characters and to NPCs alike. Only one aid die
and one interference die per source, and the fiction must carry it.
