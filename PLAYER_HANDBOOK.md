# StoneSys Player Handbook

This handbook explains how to play StoneSys from the player side. You do not need to know any other game system to use it.

## 1. Starting Play

StoneSys is a tabletop roleplaying game. Each player controls one main character. The GM describes the world, portrays everyone else, presents danger and opportunity, and says what happens when the rules call for GM judgment.

Most play is conversation.

1. The GM describes the situation.
2. You ask questions if anything is unclear.
3. You say what your character does.
4. If the outcome is obvious, the GM says what happens.
5. If the action is risky, uncertain, and important, the table uses a move or a check.
6. The result changes the situation, and play continues.

You need:

- A character sheet or web sheet.
- The campaign's home sheet, also called the steading, bastion, den, camp, or community sheet.
- Narrative dice, or the web roller.
- A few ordinary dice for damage, usually d6, d8, and d10.
- Tokens for Story Points.
- A place to track HP, XP, conditions, holds, and campaign tracks.

The group chooses one campaign before making characters. The default campaign is Stonetop. Chapter 17 has alternate campaign frames.

## 2. Characters

Your character sheet has several parts.

**Playbook** is your character type. It gives you your core identity, moves, starting gear, and ties to the campaign.

**Name, look, and duty** describe who you are, what people see when they look at you, and what pressure or responsibility follows you.

**Stats** are your core ratings:

- STR: strength, force, heavy labor, close violence.
- DEX: speed, stealth, agility, precision.
- CON: endurance, survival, pain, sickness, exhaustion.
- INT: learning, craft, logic, old lore, plans.
- WIS: awareness, instinct, judgment, spirits, wild things.
- CHA: charm, presence, command, lies, performance.

Stats are rated 1 to 5. The number is the base number of green dice you roll when a move uses that stat.

**Vitals** are the numbers that keep you in play:

- HP: how much physical harm you can take.
- Damage die: what you roll when you hurt someone or something.
- Load: how much you can carry before gear slows you down.
- Armor or protection: what reduces incoming harm when the fiction supports it.
- XP: experience points used for advancement.

**Moves** are your special rules. They describe things you can do, permissions you have, and ways the dice behave when you take certain actions.

**Gear** is what you carry. Gear matters because it changes what is possible in the fiction. A rope lets you climb where bare hands cannot. A forged writ opens doors that a smile cannot. A shield matters when a spear comes at you.

**Bonds and community ties** connect you to other characters and to the home you are trying to protect, exploit, escape, or transform.

**Tracks** measure important relationships and pressure: a patron's Bond, Debt, and Heat; a god's Devotion, Favor, and Wrath; a home base's Trouble; or a personal danger like Corruption or Defilement.

## 3. Making A Character

Make characters together.

1. Choose the campaign.
2. Choose a playbook.
3. Read the playbook's concept and duty.
4. Assign the stat array: 4, 3, 3, 2, 2, 1. Use the playbook default or swap two stats if the sheet allows it.
5. Choose name, look, background, and any campaign-specific details.
6. Take the required signature and fixed moves.
7. Choose your starting choice moves.
8. Choose gear and special possessions.
9. Fill in bonds with other player characters.
10. Fill in your community tie.
11. Mark any starting tracks listed on your sheet.
12. Introduce your character to the table.

When you introduce your character, say enough for the other players to use:

- Name and pronouns.
- What you look like.
- What people know you for.
- What you do for the group or the home community.
- One thing you want.
- One thing that could get you into trouble.

## 4. The Basic Conversation

Say what your character does as if the scene were happening in front of everyone.

Do not start with "I roll DEX" or "I use a move." Start with the action:

- "I slip through the kitchen door while the guards argue."
- "I put my hand on the fevered child's brow and call on the river saint."
- "I draw steel and hold the bridge."
- "I ask the quartermaster what she is hiding from us."

The table then decides whether a move triggers. A move triggers when your described action matches its trigger.

If no move triggers and there is no serious uncertainty, the GM says what happens. You do not roll for ordinary chores, obvious outcomes, or actions with no meaningful pressure.

Before a roll, make the situation clear:

- What are you trying to accomplish?
- How are you doing it?
- What tools, allies, leverage, or position do you have?
- What danger, opposition, cost, or time pressure is present?
- What happens if this goes badly?

The answers decide which move applies, which stat is used, how difficult the roll is, and whether boost or setback dice are added.

## 5. Dice, Pools, And Results

StoneSys uses colored dice with symbols. Roll all dice in the pool together, cancel opposing symbols, then read the result.

### Dice

| Die | Color | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ability | Green | Raw capability from your stat. |
| Proficiency | Yellow | Trained or exceptional capability. |
| Boost | Blue | A temporary edge. |
| Difficulty | Purple | How hard the task is. |
| Challenge | Red | Serious danger, opposition, or volatility. |
| Setback | Black | A temporary hindrance. |

### Symbols

Success and failure decide whether the action works.

Advantage and threat decide what else happens around the action.

Triumph is a major favorable turn. It also counts as one success.

Despair is a major unfavorable turn. It also counts as one failure.

Cancel symbols in pairs:

- One failure cancels one success.
- One threat cancels one advantage.
- Triumph and despair count for success or failure, but the triumph or despair event itself still happens.

### Building The Pool

Start with the move or check.

1. Take green dice equal to the stat being used.
2. If you have the move or are trained for this action, upgrade one green die to yellow.
3. If you have two ranks, upgrades, or mastery, upgrade a second green die to yellow.
4. The positive side can never exceed five dice.
5. You can never roll more than two yellow dice.

The GM sets difficulty:

| Difficulty | Dice |
| --- | --- |
| Simple | 0 purple |
| Easy | 1 purple |
| Average | 2 purple |
| Hard | 3 purple |
| Daunting | 4 purple |

Most moves are risky. On a risky roll, the pool must include at least one yellow die and at least one red die. If the pool does not already have them, upgrade one green to yellow and one purple to red. Red dice can never exceed two.

Add blue boost dice for temporary advantages:

- Help from an ally.
- Good tools.
- High ground.
- Surprise.
- Clear leverage.
- Preparation that directly applies.

Add black setback dice for temporary problems:

- Bad light.
- Unstable footing.
- Missing tools.
- A hostile crowd.
- Being watched.
- Exhaustion, fear, injury, or confusion.

Story Points can upgrade dice. A player-side Story Point upgrades one green die to yellow. A GM-side Story Point upgrades one purple die to red. If the upgrade would exceed the yellow or red cap, add a blue or black die instead.

### Reading The Roll

Read the roll in this order.

1. Count net successes. If at least one success remains, the action succeeds. If no successes remain, the action fails.
2. If the action fails, mark 1 XP unless the move says otherwise.
3. Apply any triumphs.
4. Apply any despairs.
5. Count net advantage or net threat.
6. Use the move's result text first.
7. If the move does not cover everything, use the default spends in chapter 16.

Common outcomes:

| Result | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Success with advantage | You do it and gain an extra benefit. |
| Success with no net advantage or threat | You do it cleanly. |
| Success with threat | You do it, but there is a cost, danger, delay, or complication. |
| Failure with advantage | You do not get what you wanted, but something useful still comes from the attempt. |
| Failure with no net advantage or threat | You miss and the GM makes a move. |
| Failure with threat | You miss and things get worse. |
| Triumph | A major favorable turn happens, even if the main action fails. |
| Despair | A major unfavorable turn happens, even if the main action succeeds. |

### One Roll Example

You try to talk your way past a suspicious guard. The move uses CHA. Your CHA is 4, so you start with four green dice. You have the move, so you upgrade one green to yellow: three green and one yellow.

The GM says the difficulty is average, so the negative side starts with two purple dice. The roll is risky, so one purple upgrades to red: one purple and one red.

You have forged papers, so the GM adds one blue die. You are covered in road dust and blood, so the GM also adds one black die.

You roll everything. If you succeed with threat, the guard lets you pass but remembers your face. If you fail with advantage, the guard blocks you, but you learn who gave the order to stop travelers.

## 6. Moves

Moves tell you when a special rule applies. Every move begins from the fiction.

### Rollable Moves

A rollable move has:

- A trigger.
- A stat.
- A default difficulty.
- Result text for success, failure, advantage, threat, triumph, or despair.

To use it:

1. Describe your action.
2. Confirm that the trigger applies.
3. Build the pool.
4. Roll.
5. Read the result against the move.
6. Say what the result looks like in the scene.

If the move gives a menu, choose from that menu when the result tells you to. If the move says the GM chooses, the GM chooses.

### Static Moves

A static move does not roll. It is a standing permission, fact, cost, or special option.

Examples:

- You can always find a buyer for stolen goods in your home quarter.
- When you spend 1 Favor, ask for an omen.
- You may take 1 Debt to call on your patron's name.
- While you protect someone with your shield, they gain cover from arrows.

When the trigger happens, follow the text.

### Hold

Hold is a temporary named currency created by a move. Different moves give different hold names: Strings, Momentum, Focus, Favor, Quarry, Reverie, or something else.

When you gain hold, write it down or increase the counter on the sheet. When you spend hold, reduce it by 1 and choose one of that move's spend options.

Hold belongs to its move. Do not spend hold from one move on another move unless a rule says you can.

### Helping And Hindering

You can help another character when your position, bond, gear, or expertise makes the help believable. Describe what you do. If the table accepts it, give that character one blue die.

Helping puts you in the situation. If the roll produces threat or despair, the GM can spend some of it against you.

You can hinder someone when you are in position to interfere. Describe how you interfere. If it works, impose one black die. You also accept the danger of getting in the way.

### Gear And Position

Gear does not usually give a permanent bonus. Gear changes what actions are possible and what dice make sense.

A proper tool can add a blue die, remove a black die, or allow an action that would otherwise be impossible. Missing gear can add a black die, increase difficulty, or make the action impossible until you change the situation.

Consumables such as arrows, torches, food, water, reagents, remedies, and bribes usually run out when threat, despair, time, or the fiction says they do.

## 7. Harm, Recovery, And Pressure

When the fiction says you are hurt, mark HP damage. Damage usually comes from a damage die. Armor or protection reduces harm when it applies.

When you hurt someone else, roll your damage die if the fiction says your attack lands. The dice pool decides whether the action succeeds. The damage die handles the injury.

At 0 HP, you are at the mercy of the situation. A playbook move may give you a desperate option. Otherwise the GM says what happens: collapse, capture, a lasting wound, death, or a chance for someone else to save you.

Conditions are ongoing problems. A condition names what is wrong and which rolls it affects.

Examples:

- Wounded: add one black die to STR and DEX rolls until treated.
- Exhausted: add one black die to CON and WIS rolls until you rest.
- Shaken: add one black die to CHA and WIS rolls until you steady yourself.
- Sickened: add one black die to CON and INT rolls until cured.

Recovery follows the fiction. Rest, shelter, medicine, healing moves, divine aid, an infirmary, a healer, or a bastion order can restore HP or remove conditions.

Some campaigns use extra pressure such as strain, corruption, defilement, heat sickness, fear, or psychic stress. Use the playbook or campaign rule that names the pressure.

## 8. XP And Advancement

Mark 1 XP when you fail a roll, unless the move says not to.

You may also mark XP when:

- A move tells you to.
- The table awards end-of-session XP.
- A bond changes or resolves.
- Your duty creates trouble and you face it.
- The campaign sheet gives an XP trigger.

Spend 5 XP to take one advancement, then clear those 5 XP.

Advancements commonly let you:

- Take a new move.
- Improve or expand a move you already have.
- Gain a new option from your campaign.
- Strengthen a bond, follower, companion, or community tie.
- Increase a stat, if the advancement list allows it.

Stat increases are rare. No stat can rise above 5.

## 9. Story Points

Story Points are shared table currency. Keep two tokens in a visible place for a small table. At larger tables, use one token per player if the group wants more movement.

Each Story Point is on either the player side or the GM side. When one side spends a point, it flips to the other side.

Players can spend a player-side Story Point to:

- Upgrade one green die to yellow on their roll.
- Declare a small true detail that fits the scene.

The GM can spend a GM-side Story Point to:

- Upgrade one purple die to red on a roll.
- Introduce a small true detail that fits the scene.

Detail declarations should be useful but not overwhelming.

Good declarations:

- "There is a back stair."
- "I know the guard from market days."
- "I packed a coil of cord."
- "This shrine has a side door for priests."

Bad declarations:

- "The villain is dead."
- "The vault is already open."
- "The army goes home."
- "The god owes me anything I ask."

When the yellow or red cap blocks an upgrade, the Story Point adds a blue die for players or a black die for the GM instead.

## 10. Patrons

A patron is a powerful backer. A patron can be a noble house, guild, temple, warlord, merchant league, hidden teacher, spirit, crime boss, rebel cell, demon, dragon, ancient order, or distant ruler.

Patrons give access, shelter, work, protection, favors, resources, secrets, and trouble.

Every patron has:

- Name.
- Type.
- Agenda.
- Public face.
- Hidden want.
- Gift.
- Price.
- Taboo.
- Enemies.
- Current demand.
- Bond.
- Debt.
- Heat.

### Bond

Bond measures how reliable your relationship with the patron is.

| Bond | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | Known contact. The patron recognizes you. |
| 1 | Useful asset. The patron spends minor resources on you. |
| 2 | Trusted agent. The patron gives serious aid and sensitive work. |
| 3 | Marked favorite. The patron treats your success as part of their own power. |

Raise Bond when you complete demands, protect the patron's interests at real cost, bring rare treasure, or expand their influence.

Lower Bond when you refuse demands, embarrass the patron, aid a rival, break a taboo, or damage the patron's agenda.

### Debt

Debt measures what the patron can call in.

Debt runs from 0 to 6. When Debt reaches 6, the patron makes a major demand immediately. After that demand is accepted or resolved, Debt resets to 3.

Add Debt when:

- You ask for help without paying now.
- You invoke the patron in danger.
- You use the patron's name to gain access.
- You fail a patron move.
- You keep a gift after refusing its price.

Clear Debt when:

- You complete a demand.
- You pay a costly tithe, secret, service, hostage, oath, or treasure.
- You accept lasting fallout for the patron.

### Heat

Heat measures attention from rivals, authorities, enemies, and suspicious locals.

| Heat | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | Quiet. |
| 1 | Rumors. |
| 2 | Watched. |
| 3 | Targeted. |
| 4 | Open retaliation. |

Heat rises when patron business becomes public, when you act violently or dramatically, or when rivals are provoked. Heat falls through time, bribes, diplomacy, scapegoats, or decisive victories.

### Asking A Patron For Aid

When you ask a patron for immediate aid, say what you want and what leverage you have.

Build the pool:

- Positive: green dice equal to Bond. Add one blue die if a relevant asset, secret, gift, or connection helps.
- You may add 1 Debt to upgrade one green die to yellow.
- Negative: the GM sets difficulty. Modest requests are average. Dangerous requests are hard. Requests that expose or weaken the patron are daunting.
- Add one black die per current Heat.

On success with advantage, choose two:

- The aid arrives quickly.
- The aid is exactly what you asked for.
- No new Debt is added.
- Heat does not rise.
- The patron reveals useful extra information.

On plain success, choose one from that list and one cost from the threat list.

On success with threat, choose one from that list. The GM also chooses one:

- Add 1 Debt.
- Add 1 Heat.
- The aid is partial, delayed, or compromised.
- The patron attaches a demand.
- A rival learns about the request.

On failure, the patron may still answer if that makes play interesting, but the GM makes a hard move. Add 1 Debt and 1 Heat, or mark the patron as offended.

## 11. Gods And Sacred Powers

A god is a power of worship, fear, place, people, or principle. A god might be a named deity, ancestor host, saint, demon-god, river power, city cult, dead empire's idol, or force of the world.

Every god has:

- Name.
- Titles.
- Portfolio.
- Symbols.
- Holy places.
- Tenets.
- Taboos.
- Favored offerings.
- Worshippers.
- Priesthood style.
- Rival powers.
- Omens.
- Wrath.

### Devotion

Devotion measures your standing with a god.

| Devotion | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | Occasional worshipper. |
| 1 | Sworn faithful. |
| 2 | Favored servant. |
| 3 | Chosen instrument. |

Gain Devotion when you uphold a tenet at cost, complete a holy task, make a meaningful offering, protect the god's people, restore a shrine, or defeat a rival power.

Lose Devotion when you break a taboo, profane a holy place, ignore a direct omen, aid a hated rival, or use divine gifts selfishly.

### Favor

Favor is divine hold. You can hold up to 3 Favor from one god at a time.

Gain 1 Favor when you complete a rite, sacrifice treasure, fulfill a vow, or roll success with advantage on a prayer.

Spend 1 Favor to:

- Add one blue die to a check that clearly serves the god's portfolio.
- Ask the GM for an omen.
- Turn success with threat on a holy act into success with advantage.
- Protect an ally from a minor curse, fear, or spiritual harm.
- Declare that a small religious contact, shrine, or sign exists nearby.

### Wrath

Wrath measures divine displeasure.

| Wrath | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | No wrath. |
| 1 | Bad omens. |
| 2 | Blessings fail or become unreliable. |
| 3 | The god sends agents, dreams, curses, or monsters. |
| 4 | The character is excommunicated, cursed, hunted, or spiritually marked. |

Reduce Wrath through confession, restitution, pilgrimage, sacrifice, completing a hard command, or making peace with the offended power.

### Praying For Aid

When you pray for divine aid under pressure, name the god, state the request, and offer something meaningful.

Build the pool:

- Positive: green dice equal to Devotion.
- Add one blue die if a relevant offering, rite, holy place, or relic helps.
- You may spend 1 Favor to upgrade one green die to yellow.
- Negative: the GM sets difficulty, usually average or hard.

On success with advantage, the god grants a clear sign or useful intervention. Choose one:

- Healing or relief from a condition.
- Protection from a specific danger.
- Revelation of a hidden truth.
- A weapon, tool, or spell empowered for one scene.
- A hostile spirit, undead, curse, or demon forced back.
- A path opened where none seemed available.

On success with threat, the aid comes with a condition. Choose one:

- Mark 1 Favor owed as a vow.
- The effect is brief or incomplete.
- The god demands visible obedience now.
- A rival power notices.
- The answer is symbolic and must be interpreted.

On failure, the god is silent, offended, or terrifyingly present. The GM makes a hard move and may mark Wrath.

## 12. Bastions, Steadings, And Home

A bastion is a player-connected home base. Depending on the campaign, it might be a village, war camp, den, hidden cellar, shrine, caravan, tower, ship, guildhouse, or frontier settlement.

A bastion has:

- Name.
- Location.
- Look.
- Founder or owner.
- Purpose.
- Scale.
- Safety.
- Prosperity.
- Influence.
- Supply.
- Trouble.
- Facilities.
- Hirelings.
- Patrons.
- Enemies.
- Strange resource, if any.

### Ratings

Bastion ratings usually run from -1 to +3.

- Scale: size, reach, and population.
- Safety: walls, guards, secrecy, law, and defensibility.
- Prosperity: money, trade, harvest, craft, and comfort.
- Influence: reputation, political reach, faith, fear, and alliances.
- Supply: stores, tools, food, medicine, and repair capacity.

### Facilities

A facility is a useful part of the bastion. Each facility has a function, a rating it uses, something it does especially well, and a vulnerability.

Examples:

- Barracks.
- Shrine.
- Workshop.
- Library.
- Infirmary.
- Watchtower.
- Market.
- Garden.
- Dock.
- Vault.
- Hidden room.
- Mine.
- Training yard.

### Trouble

Trouble measures pressure on the bastion.

| Trouble | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | Stable. |
| 1 | Complaints, shortages, rumors. |
| 2 | Open problem. |
| 3 | Crisis. |
| 4 | Disaster. |

At Trouble 4, the GM makes a major bastion move. After the crisis changes the situation, Trouble resets to 2.

### Bastion Turns

Take a Bastion Turn when a week passes, after an expedition, during downtime, or when the GM says the wider world has moved.

On a Bastion Turn:

1. Choose one order.
2. Choose one facility to lead the order.
3. Build a pool from the relevant rating.
4. Roll against average difficulty unless the situation says otherwise.
5. Apply the result.
6. The GM advances one external pressure.

If the relevant rating is negative, roll no green dice for that rating and add one black die.

On success with advantage, the order succeeds and gains an extra benefit.

On success with threat, the order succeeds but costs something.

On failure, the order fails or succeeds at a serious price, and the GM makes a hard move against the bastion.

### Common Orders

| Order | Rating | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Build | Supply | Add, repair, or upgrade a facility. |
| Craft | Prosperity | Produce gear, tools, medicine, or trade goods. |
| Gather | Supply | Stock resources. |
| Recruit | Influence | Attract hirelings, guards, specialists, or settlers. |
| Research | Influence or Prosperity | Learn lore, maps, weaknesses, rituals, or formulas. |
| Trade | Prosperity | Convert goods into coin, favors, or rare supplies. |
| Patrol | Safety | Reduce danger, scout threats, or protect roads. |
| Hide | Safety | Conceal people, treasure, fugitives, or forbidden work. |
| Heal | Supply | Recover wounds, disease, curses, morale, or conditions. |
| Celebrate | Influence | Reduce unrest, gain rumors, or create bonds. |
| Petition | Influence | Ask a patron, god, court, guild, or faction for support. |

### Fortunes And Surplus

Some steadings use Fortunes and Surplus.

Fortunes is the home community's confidence, luck, cohesion, and health. Roll Fortunes for seasonal pressure or broad community action.

Surplus is the home community's stored abundance. Spend it to survive winter, fund projects, trade, or answer crisis.

When Surplus is gone and need remains, the GM makes a move against the community.

## 13. Expeditions And Downtime

An expedition is a dangerous trip away from home with a goal.

Before leaving, settle:

- Where are you going?
- What do you want?
- Who is going?
- What route do you know?
- What danger is expected?
- What gear, food, water, mounts, guides, maps, or favors are you bringing?
- Who or what is left vulnerable while you are gone?

During travel, the GM presents danger, discovery, delay, depletion, and choices. Roll when an action is risky and important.

Good expedition goals:

- Stop a threat.
- Rescue someone.
- Recover a relic or resource.
- Make contact with a faction.
- Scout a route.
- Hunt a beast.
- Break a curse.
- Secure food, water, medicine, or shelter.
- Follow a personal duty.

When you return home:

1. Say what changed because of the expedition.
2. Recover if you can.
3. Update HP, conditions, gear, debts, heat, wrath, trouble, bonds, and holds.
4. Resolve patron demands, divine vows, and community fallout.
5. Take downtime if time passes.
6. Take a Bastion Turn if the rules call for one.

Downtime is time between immediate dangers. Use it to heal, craft, train, gather rumors, repair gear, pursue relationships, answer duties, make offerings, pay debts, reduce heat, work on a project, or support the home community.

## 14. Stonetop Campaign

Stonetop is the default campaign. The characters are important people tied to a hard-pressed home settlement. You leave home because something matters enough to risk the road, the wilds, the ruins, and the dark. You return because home is the center of the campaign.

Use Stonetop when the group wants:

- A persistent home community.
- Characters with local duties and relationships.
- Expeditions that change home life.
- Seasons, shortages, neighbors, old ruins, and dangerous frontiers.
- Followers, companions, bonds, and family pressure.

The campaign uses:

- The steading sheet.
- Community ratings.
- Fortunes and Surplus.
- Trouble.
- Seasonal pressure.
- Expeditions.
- Personal duties.
- Bonds with other PCs and local NPCs.

### Stonetop Playbooks

- Blessed: a holy or spirit-touched figure tied to old powers and rites.
- Fox: a clever troublemaker, scout, thief, smuggler, or social operator.
- Heavy: a dangerous protector with the strength to stand in the breach.
- Judge: a bearer of law, oath, judgment, and public consequence.
- Lightbearer: a servant of mercy, light, hope, and sacred duty.
- Marshal: a leader of armed folk, crews, patrols, and defense.
- Ranger: a hunter, tracker, scout, guide, and beast-bonded wanderer.
- Seeker: a keeper of lore, relics, secrets, and old dangers.
- Would-be Hero: a young or unproven figure marked by destiny, courage, or reckless purpose.

### Playing Stonetop

At character creation, make sure every character has a clear answer to:

- Why do you care about the settlement?
- Who there matters to you?
- What work do you do when not adventuring?
- What duty follows you?
- What would make you leave home in a hurry?

During play, expect expeditions to create home consequences. A successful expedition may bring food, allies, knowledge, medicine, relics, safety, or hope. A failed or costly expedition may bring wounds, enemies, omens, scarcity, grief, or a new threat at the edge of the settlement.

## 16. Reference Tables

### Default Advantage Spends

| Advantage | Spend |
| --- | --- |
| 1-2 | Add one blue die to the next allied roll that follows from this. |
| 1-2 | Notice a useful detail. |
| 1-2 | Avoid a minor cost. |
| 1-2 | Move to a slightly better position. |
| 3+ | Gain 1 hold from the move, if appropriate. |
| 3+ | Create an opening for an ally. |
| 3+ | Reduce Heat, Trouble, or Wrath by 1 if the action directly supports that. |
| 3+ | Finish faster, cleaner, or more quietly than expected. |

### Default Threat Spends

| Threat | Spend |
| --- | --- |
| 1-2 | Add one black die to a related roll soon. |
| 1-2 | Use up a minor consumable. |
| 1-2 | Draw attention. |
| 1-2 | Separate someone from gear, cover, or position. |
| 3+ | Add 1 Heat, Debt, Wrath, Trouble, or similar pressure. |
| 3+ | Break or lose important gear. |
| 3+ | Put someone in immediate danger. |
| 3+ | Reveal that the situation is worse than it looked. |

### Triumph

Use a triumph for a major favorable turn:

- A new ally appears.
- The opposition exposes a weakness.
- The action has a lasting benefit.
- A dangerous clock is delayed or stopped.
- You gain a rare opportunity.
- The move's triumph text happens.

### Despair

Use a despair for a major unfavorable turn:

- A hidden enemy is revealed.
- A resource is lost.
- A patron, god, faction, or monster takes notice.
- A crisis begins now.
- Someone is separated, captured, cursed, or marked.
- The move's despair text happens.

### Patron Favors

- Shelter.
- Legal cover.
- Forged papers.
- Rumors.
- Safe passage.
- A specialist hireling.
- Armed backup.
- A minor magic item.
- Temporary curse relief.
- Access to high society.
- A distracted rival.
- A secret revealed.
- Support for one bastion turn.

### Patron Demands

1. Recover a stolen object.
2. Silence a witness.
3. Protect a shipment.
4. Spy on a rival.
5. Escort a dangerous guest.
6. Break a taboo to prove loyalty.
7. Deliver tribute.
8. Publicly endorse the patron.
9. Sabotage a rival project.
10. Hide someone being hunted.
11. Enter a ruin for an unnamed prize.
12. Accept a mark, oath, brand, geas, or visible token.

### Divine Offerings

| Offering | Usual Use |
| --- | --- |
| Coin, gems, or treasure | Gain Favor if the amount matters. |
| Food, wine, incense, or candles | Seek an omen or minor blessing. |
| Blood, pain, fasting, or ordeal | Ask for protection, cleansing, or power. |
| Vow, oath, or public service | Gain Devotion if fulfilled. |
| Relic, enemy trophy, or reclaimed shrine | Reduce Wrath or raise Devotion. |

### Bastion Extra Benefits

On success with advantage for a Bastion Order, choose one:

- Gain 1 Supply.
- Gain 1 Coin.
- Gain 1 Favor with a patron, god, or faction.
- Reduce Heat, Trouble, or Wrath by 1.
- Complete the work faster than expected.
- A hireling, neighbor, or ally improves.
- Learn a useful secret.
- Create an opening for the next adventure.

### Bastion Costs

On success with threat for a Bastion Order, choose one:

- Spend 1 Supply.
- Spend 1 Coin.
- Add 1 Trouble.
- Add 1 Heat.
- A facility is strained and cannot lead the next Bastion Turn.
- A hireling is injured, offended, missing, or compromised.
- A patron demands credit or payment.
- A rival copies, blocks, or exploits the work.

### Downtime Options

- Rest and recover HP.
- Treat a condition.
- Repair gear.
- Craft something.
- Train or practice.
- Gather rumors.
- Make offerings.
- Visit a patron.
- Pay down Debt.
- Reduce Heat.
- Work on a bastion order.
- Strengthen a bond.
- Recruit help.
- Research a threat.
- Prepare for an expedition.

## 17. Wilder Options

These campaign frames use the same core rules, but they change the home base, pressure tracks, available playbooks, and tone of play.

### The Free Legion

The Free Legion is an escaped army on the move. The characters are part of a freed host trying to survive pursuit, thirst, factional strain, and the question of what freedom becomes after the chains are broken.

The home base is a moving war camp in scrub and broken rock. It has a forge-wagon, a field-infirmary, a little true metal, too little water, and too many enemies.

Use this campaign when the group wants:

- Survival under heat, thirst, and pursuit.
- A mobile bastion.
- Scarce metal and fragile gear.
- Patron pressure from houses, raiders, kings, and hidden allies.
- Elemental bargains.
- Psionics and mental strain.
- Defiling magic as a powerful but scarring choice.
- A community trying to become a people.

Playbooks:

- The Defiler: fast, destructive magic that scars the world.
- The Elven Dune-Raider: speed, raids, trade, distance, and tribal oaths.
- The Elemental Cleric: bargain-bound service to a raw element.
- The Mul Gladiator: arena-bred strength, endurance, and violence.
- The Poison-Bard: charm, performance, spying, and poison.
- The Preserver: forbidden magic drawn carefully from the living world.
- The Psionicist: disciplined inner power and mental force.
- The Templar: borrowed authority, city law, and dangerous old loyalties.
- The Trader-Thief: smuggling, contacts, theft, and house ties.

Important tracks and pressures:

- Bond, Debt, and Heat with patrons.
- Devotion, Favor, and Wrath for elemental powers.
- Defilement for destructive magic.
- Bastion Trouble for the moving camp.
- Supply and water pressure.

### The Hollow-Oak Holt

The Hollow-Oak Holt is a small tribe living in and around a vast old tree. The characters protect kin, memory, hunting grounds, old magic, and one another as winter grows harder and outsiders press closer.

The home base is the Holt: dens, platforms, wolf trails, a shaping grove, and a living shelter that is also the heart of the tribe.

Use this campaign when the group wants:

- A tight tribal community.
- Personal bonds as the main pressure system.
- Animal companions and kinship.
- Innate magic of healing, dream, wood, stone, and memory.
- Scarcity, cold, and fading old ways.
- Outsiders becoming kin, or failing to.
- Community survival without heavy patron or god tracks.

Playbooks:

- Chieftain: leadership, command, burden, and the survival of the tribe.
- Desert Elf: endurance, patience, water wisdom, and outsider perspective.
- Dreamweaver: sending, dreams, shared mind, and spiritual care.
- Healer: flesh magic, mercy, and the cost of being needed.
- Master Hunter: bow, track, hunt, and bond-wolf.
- Rockshaper: stone magic, shelter, and deep memory.
- Stargazer: lore, prophecy, history, and signs.
- Tree-Shaper: living wood, shelter, craft, and the Mother Tree.
- Troll Weaponsmith: outsider craft, arms, armor, and hard-won kinship.

Important tracks and pressures:

- Bonds and community ties.
- Trouble at the Holt.
- Supply, hunting, winter, and fire danger.
- Fading magic and memory.
- Outsider distrust.

### The Sign Of The Black Cat

The Sign of the Black Cat is a cellar-den under a failing wine-shop in a corrupt, fog-bound city. The characters are thieves, killers, scoundrels, believers, debtors, and survivors trying to stay fed, stay unhanged, and pull off a score big enough to change their lives.

The home base is the cellar-den: a strong door, a bolt-hole, a fence's corner, late rent, guild dues, and a secret way into old drains and tunnels below the quarter.

Use this campaign when the group wants:

- Street-level crime and survival.
- Guild dues, watch pressure, gangs, and rival crews.
- Patrons who always want payment.
- Small gods with sharp tempers.
- Corrupt magic and urban secrets.
- Heat from public trouble.
- A shared den that is useful, fragile, and compromised.

Playbooks:

- The Antiquarian Fence: appraisal, stolen history, buyers, and dangerous relics.
- The Back-Alley Duelist: reputation, blade work, and defending turf.
- The Brotherhood Assassin: patient murder, contracts, and guild ownership.
- The Dabbler in the Black Arts: minor sorcery with a real price.
- The Guild Beggar-Spy: invisible information networks.
- The Outlander Mercenary: hired muscle, outsider code, and independence.
- The Sanctioned Thief: licensed burglary under guild control.
- The Sewer Scavenger: drains, tunnels, hidden routes, and what lies below.
- The Zealot of the Street of Gods: bargain faith with a hungry little god.

Important tracks and pressures:

- Bond, Debt, and Heat with guilds, patrons, collectors, and powers.
- Devotion, Favor, and Wrath with city gods.
- Corruption for black arts.
- Bastion Trouble for the den.
- Guild dues, rent, watch attention, and rival claims.
