# StoneSys Player's Handbook

Welcome to StoneSys. This handbook explains how to play the game, how to read your character sheet, and how the rules govern your adventures. It assumes no prior knowledge of roleplaying games or specific systems.

---

## Chapter 1: Welcome to the Table

### 1.1 The Role of the Players
In StoneSys, you play as a protagonist in a shared story. You choose a "playbook" (your character class or archetype), name your character, define their appearance, and describe their actions. Your role is to portray a compelling character, take risks, connect with the other player characters, and play to find out what happens.

### 1.2 The Role of the Game Master (GM)
One participant is the Game Master. The GM does not play a single character; instead, they portray the world. The GM describes the surroundings, plays the roles of NPCs (Non-Player Characters) like merchants, allies, or monsters, and adjudicates the rules. The GM establishes situations, asks what you do, and narrates the consequences of your actions.

### 1.3 What You Need to Play
- **A Playbook Sheet**: The character sheet for your chosen archetype, which tracks your stats, moves, and resources.
- **Narrative Dice**: A set of custom symbol dice (or the virtual roller on the interactive web sheet).
- **Damage Dice**: Standard six-, eight-, or ten-sided dice (d6, d8, d10) used to roll for physical harm.
- **Story Point Tokens**: Two physical tokens used to track a shared narrative currency.
- **A Steading Sheet**: A communal character sheet that tracks your group's home village or camp.

## Chapter 2: The Basic Conversation

### 2.1 Play is a Conversation
The game is played as a back-and-forth dialogue:
1. **The GM sets the scene**: They describe what is happening around you.
2. **You describe your actions**: You say what your character does, says, or attempts in the fiction.
3. **The GM adjudicates**: 
   - If what you describe is simple and has no major stakes (e.g., buying bread, walking down a clear road), it succeeds automatically. The GM describes what happens next.
   - If what you describe involves risk, uncertainty, and potential consequences, a **Move** is triggered. You build a dice pool, roll, and resolve the action.
4. **The situation evolves**: The results of your description or the dice roll change the situation. The loop repeats.

### 2.2 Fiction First
StoneSys is a "fiction-first" game. You cannot press a mechanical button or announce "I use Defy Danger." You must describe your character's physical action in the scene first: *"I leap over the crumbling stone wall to dodge the falling boulder."* 

What is true in the fiction (whether you have a shield, if the ground is muddy, or if the enemy is distracted) determines:
- Whether a move triggers at all.
- Which stats you roll.
- How difficult the roll is.
- Whether you receive bonuses (Boosts) or penalties (Setbacks).

### 2.3 Combat and Movement without Rounds
In combat and action scenes, StoneSys does not use tactical grids, miniatures, or structured turn orders. 
- **Fictional Pacing**: There are no turn sequences. Action flows naturally like a conversation. You describe your movements based on what is happening in the scene.
- **Positioning and Risk**: Moving from one location to another simply happens if there is no immediate threat or barrier. However, if your movement is dangerous or contested (e.g., charging a spearman, running across an open field targeted by archers, or leaping over a flaming chasm), it is considered **Risky**.
- **Resolving Risky Movement**: Moving under threat triggers the **Defy Danger** move. You must roll a check (often using Dexterity for speed or Constitution to endure) to see if you reach your destination safely. On a failure, you fail to reach your goal or suffer immediate harm (like being struck by an arrow or falling).

## Chapter 3: The Dice System and Outcomes

### 3.1 The Custom Dice
When a move triggers, you roll a pool of custom narrative dice. The dice are divided into positive dice (which help you) and negative dice (which hinder you).

| Die Name | Color | Shape | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ability** | Green | d8 | Represents your character's raw attribute value. |
| **Proficiency** | Yellow | d12 | Represents trained competence. The only positive die that can roll **Triumph**. |
| **Boost** | Blue | d6 | Represents a temporary, fleeting fictional edge. |
| **Difficulty** | Purple | d8 | Represents the inherent complexity or difficulty of the task. |
| **Challenge** | Red | d12 | Represents active peril or severe danger. The only negative die that can roll **Despair**. |
| **Setback** | Black | d6 | Represents a temporary, fleeting fictional hindrance. |

### 3.2 Narrative Symbols
The faces of the narrative dice contain three pairs of opposing symbols:

- **Success (✱) and Failure (☓)**:
  - Successes (✱) indicate that your action works.
  - Failures (☓) cancel Successes on a one-for-one basis.
  - **Triumph (◆)** counts as one Success symbol.
  - **Despair (✶)** counts as one Failure symbol.
  - If you have **at least one net Success (✱)** remaining after cancellation, your action succeeds. If you have zero or net negative Successes, your action fails.
- **Advantage (▲) and Threat (▼)**:
  - Advantages (▲) indicate positive side effects, independent of whether your action succeeded.
  - Threats (▼) indicate negative side effects or minor complications.
  - Threats cancel Advantages on a one-for-one basis.
  - Net Advantage/Threat is categorized into three tiers: **0** (none), **1–2** (Minor), and **3+** (Major).
- **Triumph (◆) and Despair (✶)**:
  - A **Triumph (◆)** represents an exceptional stroke of good fortune.
  - A **Despair (✶)** represents a catastrophic complication.
  - **These two symbols never cancel each other or any other symbol**. They always take effect, even if the overall roll succeeds or fails.

### 3.3 Building the Dice Pool
To resolve a move, assemble your positive and negative dice:

1. **Start with Stats**: Take green Ability dice equal to the stat named in the move (1 to 5).
2. **Apply Training**: If you possess the move (either as a Signature move or a chosen move), upgrade **one green Ability die to a yellow Proficiency die**.
   - *Upgrading* a die means removing one green die from your pool and replacing it with one yellow die.
   - *Limits*: Your positive pool cannot exceed 5 dice total, and you cannot have more than 2 yellow Proficiency dice.
3. **Determine Difficulty**: The GM sets the baseline difficulty:
   - **Easy (1 Purple)**: A simple task under stress.
   - **Average (2 Purple)**: The default difficulty for most moves.
   - **Hard (3 Purple)**: A complex task with high stakes.
   - **Daunting (4 Purple)**: An exceptionally difficult feat.
4. **The Risky Rule**: If the roll is risky (and adventure moves are risky by default), the GM upgrades **one purple Difficulty die to a red Challenge die**.
   - *Limits*: The negative pool cannot have more than 2 red Challenge dice.
   - *Risky Floor*: A risky roll always forces at least 1 yellow Proficiency die and 1 red Challenge die into the pool, ensuring Triumph and Despair are always possible.
5. **Add Boosts and Setbacks**: Add blue Boost dice for fictional advantages and black Setback dice for fictional hindrances. These do not cancel each other out before rolling.

### 3.4 The Outcome Grid
After rolling and cancelling symbols, read your results against this grid:

| Net Dice Results | Outcome | Mechanical and Narrative Effect |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Success + net Advantage** | **Success, and...** | Your action succeeds. You also spend the net Advantage on benefits (e.g., move-specific menus or temporary Boosts). |
| **Success + net 0** | **Success** | Your action succeeds cleanly with no secondary effects. |
| **Success + net Threat** | **Success, but...** | Your action succeeds, but the GM spends the net Threat on a minor complication, resource drain, or loss of positioning. |
| **Failure + net Advantage** | **Miss with a silver lining** | The action fails. Mark 1 XP. The GM's response is soft, and you salvage a minor benefit by spending your Advantage. |
| **Failure + net 0 / Threat** | **Miss** | The action fails. Mark 1 XP. The GM makes a move and dictates the consequences. |
| **◆ Triumph (rolled)** | **A turn in your favor** | An extraordinary benefit occurs (e.g., finding a weakness, bypassing a requirement), even on a failure. |
| **✶ Despair (rolled)** | **A turn against you** | A disastrous complication occurs (e.g., a weapon breaks, reinforcements arrive), even on a success. |

*Failing is How You Grow*: Whenever you roll a Failure (a Miss), you must **mark 1 XP** on your character sheet. You learn from your mistakes.

## Chapter 4: The Character Sheet

Your playbook sheet tracks who your character is and what they can do.

### 4.1 Stats
Your character has six attributes, rated 1 to 5. These dictate the number of green Ability dice you roll:
- **Strength (STR)**: Raw physical power, lifting, and heavy melee weapons.
- **Dexterity (DEX)**: Speed, agility, stealth, and ranged weapons.
- **Constitution (CON)**: Physical stamina, endurance, and resisting death.
- **Intelligence (INT)**: Knowledge, research, logic, and arcane study.
- **Wisdom (WIS)**: Awareness, animal handling, and sensing spirits.
- **Charisma (CHA)**: Persuasion, negotiation, leadership, and deceit.

### 4.2 Vitals
- **Hit Points (HP)**: A measure of physical health. When HP hits 0, you face immediate death or desperate survival moves.
- **Damage Die**: The die type (d6, d8, or d10) you roll when dealing physical harm in combat.
- **Load**: The maximum weight you can carry without suffering Setback dice.
- **Experience Points (XP)**: Track your growth. Mark XP on failures or when moves instruct you to do so.

### 4.3 Moves
- **Signature Move**: A unique, bordered move that defines your archetype. It is always active and often provides a special resource.
- **Fixed Moves**: Passive rules and static abilities that are always active.
- **Choice Moves**: Custom abilities you check off during character creation or gain through advancement.

### 4.4 Embedments and Bonds
- **Embedment tracks**: Pip rows representing your standing with setting-specific factions, patrons, or gods. Clicking these pips tracks threshold penalties or benefits.
- **Bonds**: Statements connecting you to other PCs. Resolving a bond at the end of a session grants XP.
- **Community Tie**: Your character's specific connection to the steading.

### 4.5 Advancement
When you accumulate **5 XP**, you can clear them to check one advancement box in the footer of your sheet. This allows you to:
- Learn a new Choice Move.
- Gain new gear.
- In rare, advanced cases, increase a stat by 1 (up to the maximum cap of 5).

## Chapter 5: Currencies and Resources

### 5.1 Boosts, Setbacks, and Conditions
Fictional circumstances grant temporary dice:
- **Boosts (Blue d6)**: Added to your pool for temporary edges (e.g., high ground, an ally's aid, the perfect tool).
- **Setbacks (Black d6)**: Added for hindrances (e.g., slick footing, poor lighting, improvised tools).
- **Conditions**: Lasting physical or mental states (e.g., *Wounded*, *Exhausted*, *Shaken*). Each condition pairs with two stats. While a condition is active, you add **one black Setback die** to every roll using either of the paired stats until you receive treatment.

### 5.2 Stored Agency: Holds
Certain moves generate **Hold** (e.g., Strings, Momentum, Favor, Focus, Quarry).
- You earn Holds primarily by spending net Advantage on success rolls.
- You spend Holds later, one-for-one, to trigger specific mechanical effects without rolling.
- **Standard Exchange Rates**: If a move menu does not specify how to spend Advantage or Threat, use the following rates:

| Dice Result | Cost / Benefit |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1–2 Advantage (Minor)** | Sells for a minor menu option, or grants a blue Boost die to the next ally acting. |
| **3+ Advantage (Major)** | Sells for a major menu option, or grants **1 Hold** of the move's specific currency. |
| **◆ Triumph** | Activates the Triumph line, or grants 1 Hold and a minor menu option. |
| **1–2 Threat (Minor)** | The GM inflicts a minor cost: a black Setback die soon, loss of a consumable resource, or a detail turning sour. |
| **3+ Threat (Major)** | The GM inflicts a major cost: **tick an embedment track** (Debt, Heat, Wrath), lose your current position, or damage a tool. |
| **✶ Despair** | Activates the Despair line, or lets the GM make a hard move immediately. |

*Note on Consumables*: Ammunition, torches, rations, and remedies deplete when the GM spends Threat, representing supply erosion rather than spreadsheet tracking.

### 5.3 Story Points
Story Points represent narrative luck and flow back and forth across the table. The game starts with a shared pool (usually one point per player) split between the players' side and the GM's side.

- **Spending a Story Point**: On your turn, you can spend a Story Point to:
  - **Upgrade a die**: Convert one green Ability die to a yellow Proficiency die (subject to the pool limit of 2 yellow). 
  - **Declare a detail**: Introduce a minor, convenient fact into the fiction (e.g., "The guard is my cousin's friend," or "I brought a crowbar").
- **The Economy**: Once spent, the Story Point token flips to the GM's side. The GM can spend their Story Points to upgrade purple Difficulty dice to red Challenge dice or introduce complications, which in turn hands the Story Point back to the players.

### 5.4 Power-Structure Tracks
Embedment tracks represent your standing with external forces. Ticking these tracks changes your narrative permissions:
- **Bond (0–3) / Debt (0–6) / Heat (0–4)**: Tracks your standing with a powerful **Patron**. 
  - Raising Bond lets you roll more dice when invoking them.
  - Adding Debt represents favors owed; reaching Debt 6 forces you to immediately accept a major demand.
  - Heat represents attention from authorities or rivals; Heat 4 triggers open retaliation.
- **Devotion (0–3) / Favor (0–3) / Wrath (0–4)**: Tracks your standing with a **God**.
  - Favor is a spendable resource to bless checks or ask for omens.
  - Wrath represents divine displeasure; Wrath 4 triggers a curse or excommunication.
- **Standing (0–3)**: Tracks how hard your community will rally to protect or aid you.

## Chapter 6: The Community and the Steading

In StoneSys, the players' home community is a stateful character with its own sheet (the Steading sheet).

### 6.1 Community Ratings
The steading is defined by five ratings, ranging from `-1` to `+3`:
- **Scale**: The size, reach, and population of the community.
- **Safety**: The strength of the walls, guards, secrecy, and laws.
- **Prosperity**: The wealth, trade, harvest quality, and general comfort.
- **Influence**: The community's reputation, alliances, and political reach.
- **Supply**: The stores of food, tools, medicine, and raw materials.

### 6.2 The Seasonal Cycle and Fortunes
The community's morale and resilience are tracked by **Fortunes** (rated 1 to 5).
Once per season (or when significant downtime occurs), the players make a **Season Roll** to determine how the community fares:
- **The Pool**: Roll green Ability dice equal to the community's Fortunes. If a community facility directly helps with the season's work, upgrade one green to yellow.
- **Difficulty**: Set by the season (Spring is Average/2 purple, Deep Winter is Hard/3 purple or worse).
- **The Risky Rule**: Season rolls are always *Risky*, meaning the pool must include at least 1 yellow and 1 red die.
- **Windfalls & Disasters**: 
  - A **Triumph (◆)** represents a windfall (a birth, a bountiful discovery, an alliance).
  - A **Despair (✶)** represents a disaster (plague, fire, crop blight, or a raid).

### 6.3 Community Resources
- **Surplus**: The community's hold currency, earned via Advantage on Season rolls. Spent to survive winter, fund construction projects, or trade.
- **Trouble**: Tracks internal and external community friction (exactly like Heat). Trouble 4 triggers an immediate community crisis.

### 6.4 Bastion Turns and Orders
During downtime, weeks pass as **Bastion Turns**. The players choose one **Order** for the community to perform and select a **Facility** (e.g., Barracks, Shrine, Forge, Infirmary) to lead the work.
- **The Pool**: Roll green dice equal to the leading facility or community rating (e.g., using Supply for the *Build* order). If the rating is negative, roll 0 green dice but add 1 black Setback die.
- **The Orders**:
  - **Build (Supply)**: Create, repair, or upgrade a community facility.
  - **Craft (Prosperity)**: Produce gear, tools, medicine, or trade goods.
  - **Gather (Supply)**: Stock food, water, fuel, or raw materials.
  - **Recruit (Influence)**: Attract hirelings, guards, or specialists.
  - **Research (Influence/Prosperity)**: Learn lore, maps, or ancient secrets.
  - **Trade (Prosperity)**: Convert surplus goods into coin, favors, or supply.
  - **Patrol (Safety)**: Scout threats, clear nearby roads, and reduce danger.
  - **Hide (Safety)**: Conceal treasure, fugitives, or forbidden projects.
  - **Heal (Supply)**: Recover community morale, cure diseases, and treat wounds.

### 6.5 Frontier Resources
Some communities control a **Strange Resource** (e.g., glassy stones that store spells, blood-red ore, glowing amber resin).
- **Benefit**: Exploiting a strange resource grants **+1 Prosperity** while it flows.
- **Side Effect**: Whenever the community fails a roll to Gather, Craft, Trade, or Build, the GM rolls a side effect (e.g., workers falling ill, mutations, attracting monsters, or triggering local weather anomalies).

## Chapter 7: Stonetop Campaign

The Stonetop campaign centers on hearth fantasy: protecting an isolated village at the edge of the iron-age wilderness.

### 7.1 The Hearth Fantasy Setting
You play as the primary defenders and figures of Stonetop. The wilderness around you is dangerous, filled with ancient ruins, wild beasts, and hostile raiders. Resources are scarce, and the turning of the seasons brings real threats to your neighbors, friends, and family.

### 7.2 Campaign Structure
- **The Home and Expedition Loop**: Play cycles between expeditions into the wild (to gather resources, secure ruins, or defeat threats) and returning home to Stonetop to rest, recover, and guide the village's development.
- **Community Focus**: Every playbook includes a specific community hook tying your character to named NPCs in Stonetop. The consequences of your adventures directly impact the safety and prosperity of your neighbors.
- **The Steading**: Uses the standard Steading rules to track Stonetop's Scale, Safety, Prosperity, Influence, and Supply.

## Chapter 8: Wilder Options

These campaigns introduce alternative settings with unique rules systems that alter how you build pools, manage resources, and survive.

### 8.1 Lankhmar: Gritty Sword & Sorcery
Explore the fog-shrouded streets, damp alleys, and corrupt courts of the City of the Black Toga. Play is street-level, cynical, and highly transactional.

- **Patron and Guild Debt**: This setting uses the **Bond, Debt, and Heat** tracks extensively. Characters are heavily beholden to urban factions (the Thieves' Guild, the Assassins' Brotherhood, noble patrons, or sorcerous mentors). Debt triggers are common, and the watch is always watching.
- **Capricious Gods**: Gods in Lankhmar are fickle and petty. Religion is public and transactional, using the **Devotion, Favor, and Wrath** tracks. Prayers require immediate material sacrifices.
- **Gritty Street Brawls**: Combat is dangerous and fast. Street brawling rules emphasize maneuvers, positioning, and suffering Strain to perform extra actions.
- **Corrupting Sorcery**: Arcane magic is unstable. Spellcasting is risky, and failing or rolling Threat on magic checks ticks up a personal **Corruption** track, causing physical deformities or madness.

### 8.2 Dark Sun: Post-Apocalyptic Desert Survival
Athas is a dying desert world. Water is life, metal is rare, and tyrant Sorcerer-Kings rule the city-states with iron fists.

- **Environmental Hazards**: Heat, dehydration, and silt storms are constant threats. Survival checks are frequent, and extreme heat adds permanent black Setback dice to all physical rolls.
- **Metal Scarcity & Weapon Fragility**: Weapons are constructed from wood, bone, obsidian, or chitin. If you roll a major Threat (3+ ▼) or a Despair (✶) on an attack roll, your non-metal weapon breaks.
- **Defiling Magic**: Wizards can cast spells by sucking the life force out of the surrounding soil. A spellcaster can choose to **Defile** before rolling: this upgrades one green Ability die to a yellow Proficiency die, but it turns all surrounding vegetation to ash and ticks up their personal **Defilement** track.
- **Universal Psionics**: The "Way" of the mind is universal. Every character, regardless of playbook, has access to basic mental moves (Sending thoughts, sensing minds, or telekinetic nudges).

### 8.3 Elfquest: Prehistoric Tribal Fantasy
Play as tribal elves riding giant wolves and hunting through prehistoric forests, struggling against an encroaching ice age and hostile human tribes.

- **Personal Village Style**: This setting does **not** use formal power-structure tracks (no Debt, Heat, or Wrath). The community is a single, tight-knit family (e.g., the Hollow-Oak Holt). All relationships are personal, tracked purely through Bonds and your Community Tie.
- **Wolf Bonding**: The elves hunt and fight alongside giant pack wolves. You can form a bond with a companion wolf, allowing you to share the wolf's sensory moves and fight as a coordinated unit.
- **Sending Telepathy**: Elves communicate silently. You can "send" thoughts, emotions, and memories to other elves at any distance, bypassing verbal communication entirely without a roll.
- **Flesh-Shaping**: The setting's magic is innate and organic. The Healer playbook uses the shaping of living flesh and bone to mend wounds, cure diseases, and soothe pain at the cost of their own spiritual energy.
